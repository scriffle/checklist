# Why It Matters

Stress is one of the most universally experienced psychological phenomena. Every student sitting the VCE Psychology exam has experienced stress — possibly about that very exam. But stress is far more than a feeling. It is a complex **psychobiological process** — meaning it involves both psychological and biological mechanisms working together. Your body's stress response evolved to keep you alive in genuinely dangerous situations. The problem is that the same system that helped your ancestors escape predators now activates when you receive a difficult email, sit in traffic, or think about an upcoming deadline.

This chapter connects directly to everything you learned in Chapter 1. The nervous system structures you studied — the sympathetic and parasympathetic divisions, neurotransmitters, the enteric nervous system — are the very systems that produce and regulate the stress response. Stress is, in effect, the nervous system in action under pressure.

This is also where the exam gets hard. The 2023 and 2024 exams both tested stress content extensively, and student performance was notably poor on questions requiring **evaluation** of stress models and **application** to novel scenarios. In 2023, 74% of students scored zero when asked to evaluate the Transactional Model's ability to explain heart-rate changes during nightmares. In 2024, only 1% of students scored full marks on a question about coping flexibility and mindfulness meditation. These are not content failures — they are application failures. Students knew the models but could not use them to reason about unfamiliar situations. This chapter will teach you not only what the models say, but how to evaluate what they can and cannot explain.

# 1. Understanding Stress: Stressors and Stress Responses

## 1.1 Defining Stress

**Stress** is a state of physiological and psychological arousal produced by internal or external stressors that are perceived as challenging or exceeding a person's resources to cope. Notice that this definition has both a biological component (physiological arousal) and a psychological component (perception of challenge) — this is why stress is described as a **psychobiological process**.

A **stressor** is any stimulus — internal or external — that causes a stress response. Stressors are not inherently positive or negative; what matters is how the individual perceives and responds to them.

**Eustress** is a **positive** stress response characterised by feelings of motivation, excitement, alertness, and focused energy. It occurs when a person perceives a stressor as manageable and potentially beneficial. Examples include the nervous excitement before a sporting final, the anticipation of starting a new job, or the energising pressure of a tight deadline that motivates productive work.

**Distress** is a **negative** stress response characterised by anxiety, tension, anger, helplessness, or feeling overwhelmed. It occurs when a person perceives a stressor as threatening, harmful, or exceeding their capacity to cope. Examples include dread before a difficult exam, grief after a relationship breakdown, or chronic anxiety about financial pressures.

> **⚠️ Exam Alert:** Eustress and distress are types of **responses**, not types of stressors. The same stressor can produce eustress in one person and distress in another, depending on their perception and resources. A job interview might produce eustress in a well-prepared, confident candidate and distress in someone who feels underprepared. If a question asks you to distinguish eustress from distress, focus on the **response** (how the person feels and functions), not on the stressor itself.

## 1.2 Internal and External Stressors

**Internal stressors** originate **within the individual**. They include negative self-talk ("I'm going to fail"), perfectionism, illness, chronic pain, fatigue, hormonal changes (such as those during puberty or menstruation), and unrealistic personal expectations.

**External stressors** originate from the **environment**. They can be categorised by intensity:

- **Daily pressures (hassles):** traffic, homework deadlines, minor disagreements, running late
- **Life events:** moving house, starting a new school, getting married, having a baby
- **Acculturative stress:** challenges of adapting to a new culture, including language barriers, discrimination, and loss of familiar social support
- **Major stressors:** serious illness or injury, violent crime, death of a loved one, divorce
- **Catastrophes:** natural disasters (such as the 2019–20 Black Summer bushfires, the 2022 Victorian floods), pandemics, war

## 1.3 Physiological and Psychological Stress Responses

Stress produces responses across three dimensions:

**Physiological responses** are the body's automatic biological reactions: increased heart rate and blood pressure, rapid breathing, sweating, muscle tension, pupil dilation, suppressed digestion, and elevated cortisol and adrenaline levels. These are mediated primarily by the sympathetic nervous system and the HPA axis (covered in Section 2).

**Psychological responses** encompass changes in cognition, emotion, and behaviour:

- *Cognitive effects:* difficulty concentrating, racing thoughts, indecisiveness, mental "blanking," impaired memory
- *Emotional effects:* anxiety, irritability, anger, sadness, helplessness, fear
- *Behavioural effects:* withdrawal from social activities, changes in eating or sleeping patterns, substance use, aggression, procrastination

# 2. The Fight-Flight-Freeze Response and the Biology of Stress

## 2.1 The Fight-Flight-Freeze Response

The **fight-flight-freeze response** is the body's involuntary, immediate reaction to a perceived acute threat. It is controlled by the autonomic nervous system and occurs without conscious decision-making.

The process begins when the **amygdala** (a small, almond-shaped structure deep in the brain's temporal lobe that processes emotions, especially fear) detects danger — either from direct sensory input or from the brain's interpretation of a situation. The amygdala signals the **hypothalamus**, which acts as the command centre, activating the **sympathetic nervous system**.

### Fight or Flight

When the sympathetic nervous system becomes dominant, the **adrenal medulla** (the inner part of the adrenal gland, sitting on top of each kidney) releases **adrenaline** (also called epinephrine) and **noradrenaline** (norepinephrine) into the bloodstream. These hormones produce rapid physiological changes designed to prepare the body for vigorous physical action:

- Heart rate and blood pressure increase (to pump more blood to muscles)
- Breathing rate increases and bronchioles dilate (to take in more oxygen)
- Blood is redistributed from the digestive system to skeletal muscles (to fuel movement)
- Pupils dilate (to improve visual acuity)
- Sweating increases (to cool the body during exertion)
- Blood glucose levels rise (to provide energy)
- Digestion is suppressed (energy is redirected to more immediately useful functions)

These changes prepare the body to either confront the threat (**fight**) or escape from it (**flight**).

### Freeze

The **freeze response** occurs when the threat seems overwhelming, inescapable, or when the person cannot determine whether to fight or flee. Contrary to what many students assume, freeze is **not simply more sympathetic activation**. Instead, the **parasympathetic nervous system overrides the sympathetic nervous system**, producing:

- Body movements and vocalisations stop (tonic immobility)
- Heart rate decelerates
- Blood pressure drops
- Muscles may collapse or become rigid
- The person may feel "frozen," unable to move or speak

The freeze response has evolutionary value: in predator-prey situations, remaining completely still can avoid detection. In modern contexts, it is commonly experienced during traumatic events, sudden shock, or overwhelming fear.

> **⚠️ Exam Alert:** The 2023 examiner report noted that many students incorrectly identify the sympathetic NS as dominant during freeze. This is wrong. The **sympathetic NS** is dominant during **fight and flight**. The **parasympathetic NS** is dominant during **freeze**. If a question asks which division is dominant during the freeze response, the answer is parasympathetic.

## 2.2 Cortisol and the Chronic Stress Response

The fight-flight-freeze response is designed for **acute** (short-term) threats. But what happens when the stressor doesn't go away? When stress becomes chronic, a different biological system takes over: the **HPA axis**.

The **HPA axis** (Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Adrenal axis) is a slower, sustained hormonal stress response pathway:

1. The **hypothalamus** releases corticotropin-releasing hormone (**CRH**)
2. CRH stimulates the **anterior pituitary gland** to release adrenocorticotropic hormone (**ACTH**)
3. ACTH travels via the bloodstream to the **adrenal cortex** (the outer layer of the adrenal gland — distinct from the adrenal medulla)
4. The adrenal cortex releases **cortisol**

**Cortisol** is the body's primary chronic stress hormone. Its functions include increasing blood sugar levels, enhancing the metabolism of fats and proteins for energy, suppressing the immune system, and retarding tissue repair. In the short term, these effects are adaptive — they keep the body fuelled and functioning under pressure.

However, **prolonged cortisol exposure** (from chronic stress) has serious harmful effects:

- Hypertension and cardiovascular disease
- **Hippocampal shrinkage** — impairing memory formation and retrieval
- Weakened immune function — increasing vulnerability to illness
- Disrupted sleep patterns
- Increased vulnerability to anxiety and depression
- Weight gain (particularly abdominal fat)

> **⚠️ Exam Alert — Critical Distinction:** Students frequently confuse adrenaline and cortisol. Remember: **adrenaline** comes from the adrenal **medulla** and is released during **acute** stress (immediate, short-term). **Cortisol** comes from the adrenal **cortex** and is released during **chronic** stress (sustained, long-term). Both can operate simultaneously, but they serve different temporal functions and come from different parts of the adrenal gland. In 2024, students confused cortisol depletion with adrenaline depletion when answering questions about the exhaustion stage of GAS. Use the correct hormone for the correct context.

# 3. The Gut-Brain Axis (GBA)

## 3.1 What Is the Gut-Brain Axis?

The **gut-brain axis (GBA)** is a **bidirectional communication link** between the brain and the gastrointestinal (GI) tract. "Bidirectional" means that signals travel in both directions — the brain influences the gut, and the gut influences the brain.

The VCAA study design includes the GBA as "an area of emerging research" — this means you need to understand the basic principles, know its significance for stress and the nervous system, and recognise that research in this area is still developing. You do not need to have memorised every molecular pathway, but you do need to understand the key mechanisms and be able to discuss them in relation to stress.

The GBA communicates through multiple pathways:

**The vagus nerve** is the primary neural link between the gut and brain. It is the longest cranial nerve in the body, running from the brainstem to the abdomen. Approximately **80–90% of vagus nerve fibres are afferent** (carrying information from the gut to the brain), while only **10–20% are efferent** (carrying information from the brain to the gut). This means the gut sends far more information to the brain than the brain sends to the gut.

**The HPA axis** provides a hormonal link — cortisol released during stress alters the composition of gut microbiota, which in turn affects gut function and can feed back to influence brain function.

**The immune system** provides another link — approximately 70% of the body's immune tissue is gut-associated. Gut inflammation can trigger immune signals that affect brain function, mood, and behaviour.

**Microbial metabolites** are chemicals produced by gut bacteria that influence brain function. Gut microbiota produce neurotransmitters including approximately **95% of the body's serotonin**, as well as GABA and dopamine. These chemicals influence mood, anxiety, sleep, and cognitive function.

## 3.2 Gut Microbiota and Stress

**Gut microbiota** refers to the trillions of microorganisms (bacteria, fungi, viruses) living in the gastrointestinal tract. A **diverse and balanced** gut microbiota is associated with better mental wellbeing and reduced stress reactivity. **Dysbiosis** — an imbalance in gut microbiota composition — is linked to increased stress, anxiety, depression, and gastrointestinal disorders such as irritable bowel syndrome (IBS).

The relationship between gut microbiota and stress is bidirectional:

- **Stress affects the gut:** Chronic stress and elevated cortisol alter the composition of gut microbiota, reducing diversity and promoting the growth of harmful bacteria. This can lead to digestive problems, inflammation, and further disruption of normal gut function.
- **The gut affects stress:** Changes in gut microbiota composition alter the production of neurotransmitters and immune signals that travel to the brain, influencing mood, anxiety, and stress reactivity. An unhealthy gut can amplify the stress response; a healthy gut can dampen it.

### Research in Focus: Key GBA Studies

**Sudo et al. (2004):** Researchers raised mice in completely germ-free environments (no gut bacteria at all). These germ-free mice showed an **exaggerated HPA stress response** — they produced significantly more cortisol when exposed to stress compared to normal mice. When the germ-free mice were then colonised with *Bifidobacterium infantis* (a beneficial bacterium), their stress response **normalised**. This demonstrated that gut microbiota is essential for normal stress regulation.

**Bravo et al. (2011):** Mice fed the probiotic *Lactobacillus rhamnosus* showed altered GABA receptor expression in the brain and **reduced stress-related behaviour** (less anxiety, less depressive-like behaviour). Crucially, these effects **disappeared entirely when the vagus nerve was severed** — confirming that the vagus nerve is the key communication pathway through which gut bacteria influence brain function.

> **⚠️ Exam Alert:** The gut-brain axis is a flagship topic of the 2023–2027 study design — it was brand new when this study design was introduced. In 2023, the GBA was the focus of the **10-mark extended response** (the highest-value question on the exam), asking students to analyse a study on psychobiotic diets and discuss the role of gut microbiota in stress. In 2024, GBA content appeared in short-answer questions about the vagus nerve and nutritional intake as a protective factor. Expect this topic to continue appearing in future exams. Key points to master: bidirectional communication, the vagus nerve's predominantly afferent role (80–90% gut-to-brain), and the evidence that gut microbiota influence neurotransmitter production and stress reactivity.

# 4. Selye's General Adaptation Syndrome (GAS)

## 4.1 The Model

Hans Selye (1907–1982), often called the "father of stress research," developed the **General Adaptation Syndrome (GAS)** as a **three-stage biological model** of the body's response to stress. His key insight, drawn from research at McGill University in the 1930s, was that the body responds to **all stressors** with the **same general physiological pattern**, regardless of the specific nature of the stressor. He called this the **non-specificity principle** — whether the stressor is physical (extreme cold, injury) or psychological (exam pressure, relationship conflict), the body's biological response follows the same three-stage sequence.

Selye's original 1936 research involved exposing rats to a range of noxious stimuli (cold, surgical injury, excessive exercise, toxic injections). Despite the different stressors, all rats developed the same triad of physiological changes: **enlarged adrenal cortex**, **shrunken thymus gland and lymph nodes** (immune suppression), and **stomach ulcers**.

### Stage 1: Alarm Reaction

The alarm reaction is the body's initial response to the stressor. It has two sub-phases:

**Shock phase:** The body's first awareness of the stressor. Resistance **drops below normal** as the body is caught unprepared. Blood pressure and body temperature fall temporarily. There may be a brief loss of muscle tone. This phase is typically very brief.

**Counter-shock phase:** The body mobilises its defences. The **sympathetic nervous system** activates, triggering the fight-flight-freeze response. Adrenaline is released from the adrenal medulla. Resistance **rises rapidly above normal** as the body prepares to deal with the threat. Heart rate, blood pressure, and breathing rate increase. Blood sugar is mobilised for energy.

### Stage 2: Resistance

If the stressor persists beyond the initial alarm, the body enters the resistance stage. The intense arousal of the alarm stage diminishes, but the body maintains a **sustained level of physiological arousal above normal**. The adrenal cortex releases **cortisol** to maintain energy supplies and repair minor damage. Non-essential physiological processes (digestion, growth, reproduction, immune function) are suppressed to conserve resources for coping with the stressor.

During the resistance stage, the person may appear to be coping well — they are functioning and adapting to the stressor. However, the body's resources are being gradually depleted. If the stressor is removed during this stage, the body returns to homeostasis (normal functioning). If the stressor continues, the body moves to the third stage.

### Stage 3: Exhaustion

If the stressor persists long enough, the body's resources become **depleted**. Resistance **falls below normal** — even below the initial pre-stress baseline. The person becomes highly vulnerable to:

- **Physical disorders:** hypertension, cardiovascular disease, stomach ulcers, chronic fatigue
- **Immune suppression:** increased susceptibility to infections and illness
- **Psychological disorders:** anxiety, depression, burnout
- In Selye's original animal experiments, prolonged exposure ultimately resulted in **death**

### The Standard GAS Graph

You should be able to draw and interpret the standard GAS graph from memory:

- **X-axis:** Time
- **Y-axis:** Level of Resistance to Stress
- **Horizontal dashed line:** Normal resistance level

The curve shows the characteristic "dip-rise-plateau-fall" shape:

1. **Shock** — resistance dips below normal (brief)
2. **Counter-shock** — resistance rises sharply above normal
3. **Resistance** — resistance plateaus above normal (sustained)
4. **Exhaustion** — resistance falls below normal and continues declining

## 4.2 Evaluating the Explanatory Power of GAS

The VCAA study design uses the phrase "explanatory power" three times across U3 AOS1, and the 2023 examiner report explicitly stated that students should practise evaluating the explanatory power of all stress models. **Evaluating** means making a judgement about how well the model explains a phenomenon — not just describing what the model says.

### Strengths of GAS

- **First model to systematically link chronic stress to disease.** Before Selye, the connection between prolonged stress and physical illness was not well understood. GAS provided a framework for understanding how stress "gets under the skin."
- **Rich physiological detail.** GAS describes specific biological mechanisms (adrenal activation, cortisol release, immune suppression) that can be measured and verified.
- **Strong laboratory evidence.** Selye's research demonstrated consistent, replicable physiological responses across different stressors and different organisms.
- **Explains non-specificity.** The model accounts for why very different stressors (physical injury, emotional trauma, illness) can produce similar physical outcomes (hypertension, ulcers, immune compromise).

### Limitations of GAS

- **Ignores individual differences.** GAS assumes everyone responds to stress in the same biological way, in the same sequence. In reality, people vary enormously in their stress responses based on personality, past experience, perceived control, and social support.
- **Ignores psychological factors.** GAS is a purely biological model — it does not account for cognitive appraisal, perception, or meaning-making. Two people facing the same stressor may experience very different stress responses depending on how they **interpret** the situation, but GAS cannot explain this.
- **Based primarily on animal research.** Selye's foundational research used rats, raising questions about the generalisability of findings to humans, who have more complex cognitive processes.
- **Assumes a linear progression.** GAS implies that everyone who faces chronic stress will progress through alarm → resistance → exhaustion in order. In practice, people may move back and forth between stages, or never reach exhaustion.
- **Does not explain eustress.** GAS treats all stress as leading toward negative outcomes (exhaustion, disease). It cannot account for positive stress responses (eustress) or situations where stress enhances performance.

> **⚠️ Exam Alert:** When the exam asks you to **evaluate** GAS, you must make a judgement — not just list strengths and limitations. A strong evaluation connects the model's features to the specific scenario in the question and explains **why** the model can or cannot adequately account for what is described. For example: "GAS has strong explanatory power for Mei's situation because her chronic work stress has persisted for months, and her symptoms (fatigue, frequent illness, high blood pressure) are consistent with the exhaustion stage, where prolonged cortisol exposure depletes bodily resources and suppresses immune function. However, GAS cannot explain why Mei experiences more severe stress than her colleague facing the same workload, because it does not account for individual differences in cognitive appraisal."

# 5. Lazarus and Folkman's Transactional Model of Stress and Coping

## 5.1 The Model

Richard Lazarus (1922–2002) and Susan Folkman (b. 1938) proposed that stress is not simply a stimulus or a response — it arises from the **transaction** (interaction) between an individual and their environment. The model's core principle is:

**Stress occurs when perceived demands exceed perceived resources to cope.**

Unlike GAS, which focuses on universal biological responses, the Transactional Model focuses on **cognitive appraisal** — the mental processes by which a person evaluates a situation and their ability to deal with it. This is why different people can experience very different levels of stress in response to the same stressor.

The model involves two stages of appraisal:

### Primary Appraisal

**Primary appraisal** is the person's initial evaluation of the **significance** of a situation — "What does this mean for me?" There are three possible outcomes:

- **Irrelevant:** The situation has no relevance to the person's wellbeing. No stress response occurs. (Example: hearing that a road you never use is closed for repairs.)
- **Benign-positive:** The situation is perceived as favourable or positive. No stress response occurs, and the person may experience positive emotions. (Example: receiving an unexpected compliment from a teacher.)
- **Stressful:** The situation is perceived as potentially harmful, threatening, or challenging. This triggers further evaluation. Stressful appraisals are further categorised as:
  - **Harm/loss:** Damage has already occurred. (Example: you have already failed an exam.)
  - **Threat:** There is potential for future harm or loss. (Example: you are about to sit a difficult exam you feel underprepared for.)
  - **Challenge:** There is potential for growth, gain, or mastery. (Example: you see the exam as an opportunity to demonstrate your knowledge.)

### Secondary Appraisal

**Secondary appraisal** is the person's evaluation of their **coping resources and options** — "What can I do about this?" The person assesses both:

- **Internal resources:** Confidence, knowledge, skills, past experience, physical fitness, emotional resilience
- **External resources:** Money, time, social support, access to information or professional help

If the person judges that their resources are **sufficient** to meet the demands, a stress response may be minimal or absent, and the person selects an appropriate coping strategy. If the person judges that the demands **greatly exceed** their resources, a significant stress response occurs.

> **⚠️ Exam Alert — Scope Limitation:** The VCAA study design specifies "primary and secondary appraisal only." **Reappraisal** (a third step where the person re-evaluates the situation after initial coping attempts) is NOT required by the study design. Students who include reappraisal are wasting time and word count on non-examinable content. Stick to primary and secondary appraisal only.

### Standard Flowchart

You should be able to draw this from memory:

```
Stressor → PRIMARY APPRAISAL
             ├── Irrelevant → No stress
             ├── Benign-positive → No stress
             └── Stressful
                  ├── Harm/loss
                  ├── Threat        → SECONDARY APPRAISAL
                  └── Challenge       ├── Resources sufficient → Coping strategy
                                      └── Resources insufficient → STRESS RESPONSE
```

## 5.2 Evaluating the Explanatory Power of the Transactional Model

### Strengths

- **Explains individual differences.** This is the model's central strength. Because stress depends on subjective appraisal, the model can explain why two people facing the same stressor experience different levels of stress — one may appraise it as a challenge while the other appraises it as a threat.
- **Focuses on controllable factors.** Appraisal and coping resources can be modified through psychological interventions (e.g. cognitive behavioural therapy, stress management training). This gives the model practical therapeutic value.
- **Based on human research.** Unlike GAS, the Transactional Model was developed from research with human participants, enhancing its relevance to human stress experiences.
- **Led to effective interventions.** The model underpins many evidence-based stress management approaches, including cognitive restructuring (changing how a person appraises a situation) and resource-building (developing coping skills and social support).

### Limitations

- **Cannot explain stress that occurs without conscious appraisal.** This is the model's most critical limitation. Some stress responses occur **automatically**, without any cognitive evaluation. Examples include: heart-rate increases during nightmares (the person is asleep and unable to consciously appraise), startle responses to sudden loud noises, and physiological stress responses in infants (who lack the cognitive capacity for appraisal).
- **Overlooks physiological responses.** The model focuses on psychological processes (appraisal, perception) and does not adequately explain the biological mechanisms of stress (cortisol release, immune suppression, autonomic activation).
- **Difficult to test experimentally.** Appraisal is a subjective, internal process that cannot be directly observed or precisely measured. Research relies on self-report, which is vulnerable to inaccuracies.
- **Assumes sequential appraisal.** The model implies that primary appraisal occurs before secondary appraisal. In reality, these processes often occur simultaneously or in rapid alternation, making them difficult to distinguish.

> **⚠️ Exam Alert — The Nightmare Question:** In the 2023 exam, Q7b asked students to evaluate the Transactional Model's appropriateness for explaining heart-rate increases during nightmares. **74% of students scored zero.** The mistake was describing what primary and secondary appraisal would look like in a nightmare, rather than evaluating whether the model **could explain the phenomenon at all**. The correct approach: the Transactional Model has **poor explanatory power** for nightmares because (1) the person is **asleep** and therefore unable to engage in **conscious cognitive appraisal**, and (2) heart-rate changes are **physiological responses** controlled by the autonomic nervous system, which the model (as a psychological model) does not adequately address. **Evaluate** means judge — does this model work here, or doesn't it? Don't describe the model when asked to evaluate it.

# 6. Comparing GAS and the Transactional Model

Understanding the differences between these two models is essential for exam questions that ask you to compare, contrast, or evaluate them. The table below captures the key distinctions:

| Feature | GAS (Selye) | Transactional Model (Lazarus & Folkman) |
|---------|-------------|----------------------------------------|
| **Focus** | Universal biological response | Individual cognitive appraisal |
| **Type of model** | Biological | Psychological |
| **Language** | Biological: cortisol, adrenaline, alarm, resistance, exhaustion, adrenal cortex, immune suppression | Psychological: appraisal, perceived threat/challenge/harm, coping resources, perceived demands |
| **Individual differences** | Cannot explain — assumes the same response for everyone | Central feature — explains why people respond differently to the same stressor |
| **Evidence base** | Primarily animal research (rats) | Primarily human research |
| **Strengths** | Explains physiology of chronic stress; links stress to disease | Explains individual variation; identifies controllable factors |
| **Limitations** | Ignores psychology and individual differences | Cannot explain stress without conscious appraisal; ignores physiology |
| **Best at explaining** | Chronic stress leading to physical illness; physiological stress responses | Individual variation in stress responses; why perception matters |
| **Poor at explaining** | Why two people respond differently to the same stressor | Automatic physiological responses; stress in sleep, infants, or unconscious states |

> **⚠️ Exam Alert — Match Your Language to the Model:** The examiners penalise students who mix biological language with the psychological model or vice versa. When discussing GAS, use biological terms: cortisol, adrenaline, adrenal cortex, adrenal medulla, alarm/resistance/exhaustion, sympathetic activation, immune suppression. When discussing the Transactional Model, use psychological terms: appraisal, perceived threat/challenge/harm, coping resources, perceived demands. In the 2023 exam, only 38% of students correctly answered a multiple-choice question because the distractors used Lazarus & Folkman language in options about GAS. If you can keep the vocabularies separate, you have a significant advantage.

# 7. A Framework for Evaluating Explanatory Power

The VCAA expects you to be able to evaluate the explanatory power of GAS, the Transactional Model, and the fight-flight-freeze response across **diverse scenarios** — not just the familiar textbook examples. The 2023 examiner report explicitly recommended that students practise this skill throughout the year.

Here is a simple four-question framework you can apply to any scenario:

1. **Can this model explain what is happening in this scenario?**
2. **What can it explain well?** (Which specific features of the scenario does it account for?)
3. **What can't it explain?** (Which features of the scenario fall outside the model's scope?)
4. **Why?** (What is it about the model's focus — biological vs psychological — that creates this limitation?)

### Practice: Applying the Framework

**Scenario A: Workplace Burnout**
*Mei has been working 60-hour weeks for eight months. She is exhausted, frequently sick, and has developed high blood pressure.*

- **GAS:** Strong explanatory power. Mei's symptoms align with the exhaustion stage — prolonged stress has depleted her resources, leading to immune suppression (frequent illness), cardiovascular effects (high blood pressure), and fatigue. GAS's non-specificity principle explains why chronic work stress produces physical symptoms.
- **Transactional Model:** Moderate explanatory power. It can explain why Mei experiences stress (she may appraise her workload as exceeding her resources), but it cannot explain the specific physiological symptoms. It could explain why a colleague in the same role might cope better (different appraisal of demands vs resources), but not Mei's high blood pressure or immune suppression.

**Scenario B: Nightmares**
*Twelve-year-old Zac experiences increased heart rate and sweating during a nightmare about being chased.*

- **GAS:** Moderate explanatory power. The alarm reaction's activation of the sympathetic NS (increased heart rate, sweating) matches Zac's symptoms. However, GAS does not distinguish between conscious and unconscious experiences of stress, and it is unclear whether the full three-stage model applies to a brief nightmare.
- **Transactional Model:** Poor explanatory power. Zac is asleep and unable to engage in conscious cognitive appraisal. His heart-rate increase and sweating are involuntary physiological responses, not products of deliberate evaluation of demands versus resources. The model fundamentally requires conscious appraisal, which cannot occur during sleep.
- **Fight-flight-freeze:** Strong explanatory power. The amygdala can activate the sympathetic NS during a nightmare based on dream content, triggering fight-or-flight responses (increased heart rate, sweating) without conscious appraisal.

**Scenario C: Exam Preparation**
*Two students, Aisha and Ben, are preparing for the same VCE Psychology exam. Aisha feels motivated and focused; Ben feels overwhelmed and anxious.*

- **GAS:** Poor explanatory power. Both students face the same stressor, but their responses are completely different — one experiences eustress, the other distress. GAS predicts the same biological response for both and cannot account for this individual variation.
- **Transactional Model:** Strong explanatory power. Aisha may appraise the exam as a **challenge** (potential for mastery) and evaluate her resources as sufficient (good preparation, strong study skills). Ben may appraise it as a **threat** (potential for failure) and evaluate his resources as insufficient (feeling underprepared, lacking confidence). The difference in their stress responses is entirely explained by differences in appraisal.

# 8. Coping Strategies

## 8.1 Approach and Avoidance Strategies

**Approach coping strategies** involve directly confronting or addressing the stressor. Examples include:

- Seeking expert advice or information about the problem
- Creating a structured action plan
- Problem-solving and brainstorming solutions
- Seeking social support (talking to friends, family, or professionals)
- Directly addressing a conflict through open communication
- Developing new skills relevant to the challenge

Approach strategies are generally associated with **better long-term mental health outcomes**. People who habitually use approach coping report fewer psychological symptoms and greater perceived control over their lives.

**Avoidance coping strategies** involve evading, minimising, or disengaging from the stressor. Examples include:

- Denial (refusing to acknowledge the problem exists)
- Distraction (turning attention to unrelated activities)
- Procrastination (delaying action on the stressor)
- Fantasy or wishful thinking
- Substance use (alcohol, drugs)
- Emotional disengagement (suppressing feelings about the stressor)

> **⚠️ Exam Alert:** A common misconception — and a frequent source of lost marks — is that avoidance coping is always maladaptive. This is **not true**. Avoidance strategies can be **appropriate and effective** when the stressor is genuinely **uncontrollable**. For example, while waiting for medical test results (a stressor you cannot influence), distraction is a reasonable and healthy coping strategy. Dwelling on the results would increase anxiety without changing the outcome. The key principle is **context-specific effectiveness** (Section 8.2).

## 8.2 Context-Specific Effectiveness

**Context-specific effectiveness** means that a coping strategy's appropriateness depends on the **match between the strategy and the situation**. No strategy is universally good or universally bad — effectiveness depends on context.

The general principle is:

- **Approach strategies** are generally more effective when the stressor is **controllable** — when the person can actually do something to address or change the situation (e.g. studying for an exam, seeking treatment for a health condition, having a difficult conversation to resolve a conflict).
- **Avoidance strategies** are sometimes more appropriate when the stressor is **uncontrollable** — when the person cannot change the situation and must endure it (e.g. waiting for surgery, grieving the death of a loved one, experiencing an unavoidable natural disaster). In these situations, approach strategies may increase frustration without reducing stress.

However, even when avoidance is initially appropriate, **excessive long-term reliance** on avoidance strategies is associated with poorer mental health outcomes. A person who uses avoidance to cope with every stressor — including controllable ones — is likely to experience increased anxiety, unresolved problems, and a diminished sense of control.

## 8.3 Coping Flexibility

**Coping flexibility** is the ability to **modify, adjust, or switch coping strategies based on changing situational demands**. It involves three components:

1. **Awareness:** Recognising that the current strategy is ineffective or no longer appropriate
2. **Discontinuation:** Stopping the ineffective strategy
3. **Replacement:** Implementing a different, more suitable strategy

High coping flexibility is associated with **better mental health outcomes** — people who can adapt their coping approach to match changing circumstances experience less distress and recover more quickly from stressful events (Kato, 2012). Low coping flexibility — rigidly persisting with the same coping strategy regardless of whether it is working — is associated with depressive symptoms and prolonged distress.

**Example:** A student initially copes with exam stress by creating a detailed study schedule (approach strategy). Midway through the exam period, she realises she is burning out and switches to incorporating regular exercise and social activities (a blend of approach and avoidance). After the exams, she uses distraction and leisure activities to decompress (avoidance strategy). Her ability to recognise when each strategy is working, stop when it isn't, and try something different demonstrates high coping flexibility.

> **⚠️ Exam Alert — The 2024 Coping Flexibility Disaster:** In 2024, Q5c asked about the relationship between coping flexibility and mindfulness meditation. Only **1% of students scored full marks**, and 43% scored zero. The key insight students missed: **mindfulness meditation emphasises sustained, non-judgemental acceptance of present-moment experience** — it inherently involves committing to a single, consistent approach (acceptance) rather than switching strategies. This makes it **potentially incompatible** with coping flexibility, which requires the ability to abandon an approach and try something different. High-scoring responses explained this tension: mindfulness can be one valuable tool within a flexible coping repertoire, but mindfulness *as a sole strategy* limits flexibility. If a question asks you to evaluate coping flexibility in relation to another concept, think about whether the other concept **supports or constrains** the ability to switch strategies.

# 9. Bringing It All Together: Stress as a Psychobiological Process

The word "psychobiological" in the heading of this Area of Study is not decorative — it tells you exactly how the VCAA wants you to think about stress. Stress involves both **psychological** processes (perception, appraisal, emotional responses) and **biological** processes (sympathetic activation, cortisol release, neurotransmitter changes, gut-brain communication). Neither dimension alone captures the full picture.

This is why the study design includes **both** a biological model (GAS) and a psychological model (the Transactional Model). Each captures a different aspect of the stress experience. GAS explains the body's universal physiological response to chronic stress. The Transactional Model explains why individuals differ in their stress responses based on cognitive appraisal. The fight-flight-freeze response explains immediate, acute threat responses. The gut-brain axis reveals a newly understood pathway through which stress affects — and is affected by — the body's largest collection of microorganisms.

For the exam, the highest-scoring responses will be those that can move fluently between biological and psychological explanations, selecting the model or mechanism most appropriate to the specific scenario, and acknowledging the limitations of each. This is the essence of a psychobiological approach — understanding that mind and body are not separate systems but deeply interconnected ones.

| **Stressor** | Any stimulus — internal or external — that causes a stress response |

# Why It Matters

Stress is one of the most universally experienced psychological phenomena. Every student sitting the VCE Psychology exam has experienced stress — possibly about that very exam. But stress is far more than a feeling. It is a complex **psychobiological process** — meaning it involves both psychological and biological mechanisms working together. Your body's stress response evolved to keep you alive in genuinely dangerous situations. The problem is that the same system that helped your ancestors escape predators now activates when you receive a difficult email, sit in traffic, or think about an upcoming deadline.

This chapter connects directly to everything you learned in Chapter 1. The nervous system structures you studied — the sympathetic and parasympathetic divisions, neurotransmitters, the enteric nervous system — are the very systems that produce and regulate the stress response. Stress is, in effect, the nervous system in action under pressure.

This is also where the exam gets hard. The 2023 and 2024 exams both tested stress content extensively, and student performance was notably poor on questions requiring **evaluation** of stress models and **application** to novel scenarios. In 2023, 74% of students scored zero when asked to evaluate the Transactional Model's ability to explain heart-rate changes during nightmares. In 2024, only 1% of students scored full marks on a question about coping flexibility and mindfulness meditation. These are not content failures — they are application failures. Students knew the models but could not use them to reason about unfamiliar situations. This chapter will teach you not only what the models say, but how to evaluate what they can and cannot explain.

# 1. Understanding Stress: Stressors and Stress Responses

## 1.1 Defining Stress

**Stress** is a state of physiological and psychological arousal produced by internal or external stressors that are perceived as challenging or exceeding a person's resources to cope. Notice that this definition has both a biological component (physiological arousal) and a psychological component (perception of challenge) — this is why stress is described as a **psychobiological process**.

A **stressor** is any stimulus — internal or external — that causes a stress response. Stressors are not inherently positive or negative; what matters is how the individual perceives and responds to them.

**Eustress** is a **positive** stress response characterised by feelings of motivation, excitement, alertness, and focused energy. It occurs when a person perceives a stressor as manageable and potentially beneficial. Examples include the nervous excitement before a sporting final, the anticipation of starting a new job, or the energising pressure of a tight deadline that motivates productive work.

**Distress** is a **negative** stress response characterised by anxiety, tension, anger, helplessness, or feeling overwhelmed. It occurs when a person perceives a stressor as threatening, harmful, or exceeding their capacity to cope. Examples include dread before a difficult exam, grief after a relationship breakdown, or chronic anxiety about financial pressures.

> **⚠️ Exam Alert:** Eustress and distress are types of **responses**, not types of stressors. The same stressor can produce eustress in one person and distress in another, depending on their perception and resources. A job interview might produce eustress in a well-prepared, confident candidate and distress in someone who feels underprepared. If a question asks you to distinguish eustress from distress, focus on the **response** (how the person feels and functions), not on the stressor itself.

## 1.2 Internal and External Stressors

**Internal stressors** originate **within the individual**. They include negative self-talk ("I'm going to fail"), perfectionism, illness, chronic pain, fatigue, hormonal changes (such as those during puberty or menstruation), and unrealistic personal expectations.

**External stressors** originate from the **environment**. They can be categorised by intensity:

- **Daily pressures (hassles):** traffic, homework deadlines, minor disagreements, running late
- **Life events:** moving house, starting a new school, getting married, having a baby
- **Acculturative stress:** challenges of adapting to a new culture, including language barriers, discrimination, and loss of familiar social support
- **Major stressors:** serious illness or injury, violent crime, death of a loved one, divorce
- **Catastrophes:** natural disasters (such as the 2019–20 Black Summer bushfires, the 2022 Victorian floods), pandemics, war

## 1.3 Physiological and Psychological Stress Responses

Stress produces responses across three dimensions:

**Physiological responses** are the body's automatic biological reactions: increased heart rate and blood pressure, rapid breathing, sweating, muscle tension, pupil dilation, suppressed digestion, and elevated cortisol and adrenaline levels. These are mediated primarily by the sympathetic nervous system and the HPA axis (covered in Section 2).

**Psychological responses** encompass changes in cognition, emotion, and behaviour:

- *Cognitive effects:* difficulty concentrating, racing thoughts, indecisiveness, mental "blanking," impaired memory
- *Emotional effects:* anxiety, irritability, anger, sadness, helplessness, fear
- *Behavioural effects:* withdrawal from social activities, changes in eating or sleeping patterns, substance use, aggression, procrastination

# 2. The Fight-Flight-Freeze Response and the Biology of Stress

## 2.1 The Fight-Flight-Freeze Response

The **fight-flight-freeze response** is the body's involuntary, immediate reaction to a perceived acute threat. It is controlled by the autonomic nervous system and occurs without conscious decision-making.

The process begins when the **amygdala** (a small, almond-shaped structure deep in the brain's temporal lobe that processes emotions, especially fear) detects danger — either from direct sensory input or from the brain's interpretation of a situation. The amygdala signals the **hypothalamus**, which acts as the command centre, activating the **sympathetic nervous system**.

### Fight or Flight

When the sympathetic nervous system becomes dominant, the **adrenal medulla** (the inner part of the adrenal gland, sitting on top of each kidney) releases **adrenaline** (also called epinephrine) and **noradrenaline** (norepinephrine) into the bloodstream. These hormones produce rapid physiological changes designed to prepare the body for vigorous physical action:

- Heart rate and blood pressure increase (to pump more blood to muscles)
- Breathing rate increases and bronchioles dilate (to take in more oxygen)
- Blood is redistributed from the digestive system to skeletal muscles (to fuel movement)
- Pupils dilate (to improve visual acuity)
- Sweating increases (to cool the body during exertion)
- Blood glucose levels rise (to provide energy)
- Digestion is suppressed (energy is redirected to more immediately useful functions)

These changes prepare the body to either confront the threat (**fight**) or escape from it (**flight**).

### Freeze

The **freeze response** occurs when the threat seems overwhelming, inescapable, or when the person cannot determine whether to fight or flee. Contrary to what many students assume, freeze is **not simply more sympathetic activation**. Instead, the **parasympathetic nervous system overrides the sympathetic nervous system**, producing:

- Body movements and vocalisations stop (tonic immobility)
- Heart rate decelerates
- Blood pressure drops
- Muscles may collapse or become rigid
- The person may feel "frozen," unable to move or speak

The freeze response has evolutionary value: in predator-prey situations, remaining completely still can avoid detection. In modern contexts, it is commonly experienced during traumatic events, sudden shock, or overwhelming fear.

> **⚠️ Exam Alert:** The 2023 examiner report noted that many students incorrectly identify the sympathetic NS as dominant during freeze. This is wrong. The **sympathetic NS** is dominant during **fight and flight**. The **parasympathetic NS** is dominant during **freeze**. If a question asks which division is dominant during the freeze response, the answer is parasympathetic.

## 2.2 Cortisol and the Chronic Stress Response

The fight-flight-freeze response is designed for **acute** (short-term) threats. But what happens when the stressor doesn't go away? When stress becomes chronic, a different biological system takes over: the **HPA axis**.

The **HPA axis** (Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Adrenal axis) is a slower, sustained hormonal stress response pathway:

1. The **hypothalamus** releases corticotropin-releasing hormone (**CRH**)
2. CRH stimulates the **anterior pituitary gland** to release adrenocorticotropic hormone (**ACTH**)
3. ACTH travels via the bloodstream to the **adrenal cortex** (the outer layer of the adrenal gland — distinct from the adrenal medulla)
4. The adrenal cortex releases **cortisol**

**Cortisol** is the body's primary chronic stress hormone. Its functions include increasing blood sugar levels, enhancing the metabolism of fats and proteins for energy, suppressing the immune system, and retarding tissue repair. In the short term, these effects are adaptive — they keep the body fuelled and functioning under pressure.

However, **prolonged cortisol exposure** (from chronic stress) has serious harmful effects:

- Hypertension and cardiovascular disease
- **Hippocampal shrinkage** — impairing memory formation and retrieval
- Weakened immune function — increasing vulnerability to illness
- Disrupted sleep patterns
- Increased vulnerability to anxiety and depression
- Weight gain (particularly abdominal fat)

> **⚠️ Exam Alert — Critical Distinction:** Students frequently confuse adrenaline and cortisol. Remember: **adrenaline** comes from the adrenal **medulla** and is released during **acute** stress (immediate, short-term). **Cortisol** comes from the adrenal **cortex** and is released during **chronic** stress (sustained, long-term). Both can operate simultaneously, but they serve different temporal functions and come from different parts of the adrenal gland. In 2024, students confused cortisol depletion with adrenaline depletion when answering questions about the exhaustion stage of GAS. Use the correct hormone for the correct context.

# 3. The Gut-Brain Axis (GBA)

## 3.1 What Is the Gut-Brain Axis?

The **gut-brain axis (GBA)** is a **bidirectional communication link** between the brain and the gastrointestinal (GI) tract. "Bidirectional" means that signals travel in both directions — the brain influences the gut, and the gut influences the brain.

The VCAA study design includes the GBA as "an area of emerging research" — this means you need to understand the basic principles, know its significance for stress and the nervous system, and recognise that research in this area is still developing. You do not need to have memorised every molecular pathway, but you do need to understand the key mechanisms and be able to discuss them in relation to stress.

The GBA communicates through multiple pathways:

**The vagus nerve** is the primary neural link between the gut and brain. It is the longest cranial nerve in the body, running from the brainstem to the abdomen. Approximately **80–90% of vagus nerve fibres are afferent** (carrying information from the gut to the brain), while only **10–20% are efferent** (carrying information from the brain to the gut). This means the gut sends far more information to the brain than the brain sends to the gut.

**The HPA axis** provides a hormonal link — cortisol released during stress alters the composition of gut microbiota, which in turn affects gut function and can feed back to influence brain function.

**The immune system** provides another link — approximately 70% of the body's immune tissue is gut-associated. Gut inflammation can trigger immune signals that affect brain function, mood, and behaviour.

**Microbial metabolites** are chemicals produced by gut bacteria that influence brain function. Gut microbiota produce neurotransmitters including approximately **95% of the body's serotonin**, as well as GABA and dopamine. These chemicals influence mood, anxiety, sleep, and cognitive function.

## 3.2 Gut Microbiota and Stress

**Gut microbiota** refers to the trillions of microorganisms (bacteria, fungi, viruses) living in the gastrointestinal tract. A **diverse and balanced** gut microbiota is associated with better mental wellbeing and reduced stress reactivity. **Dysbiosis** — an imbalance in gut microbiota composition — is linked to increased stress, anxiety, depression, and gastrointestinal disorders such as irritable bowel syndrome (IBS).

The relationship between gut microbiota and stress is bidirectional:

- **Stress affects the gut:** Chronic stress and elevated cortisol alter the composition of gut microbiota, reducing diversity and promoting the growth of harmful bacteria. This can lead to digestive problems, inflammation, and further disruption of normal gut function.
- **The gut affects stress:** Changes in gut microbiota composition alter the production of neurotransmitters and immune signals that travel to the brain, influencing mood, anxiety, and stress reactivity. An unhealthy gut can amplify the stress response; a healthy gut can dampen it.

### Research in Focus: Key GBA Studies

**Sudo et al. (2004):** Researchers raised mice in completely germ-free environments (no gut bacteria at all). These germ-free mice showed an **exaggerated HPA stress response** — they produced significantly more cortisol when exposed to stress compared to normal mice. When the germ-free mice were then colonised with *Bifidobacterium infantis* (a beneficial bacterium), their stress response **normalised**. This demonstrated that gut microbiota is essential for normal stress regulation.

**Bravo et al. (2011):** Mice fed the probiotic *Lactobacillus rhamnosus* showed altered GABA receptor expression in the brain and **reduced stress-related behaviour** (less anxiety, less depressive-like behaviour). Crucially, these effects **disappeared entirely when the vagus nerve was severed** — confirming that the vagus nerve is the key communication pathway through which gut bacteria influence brain function.

> **⚠️ Exam Alert:** The gut-brain axis is a flagship topic of the 2023–2027 study design — it was brand new when this study design was introduced. In 2023, the GBA was the focus of the **10-mark extended response** (the highest-value question on the exam), asking students to analyse a study on psychobiotic diets and discuss the role of gut microbiota in stress. In 2024, GBA content appeared in short-answer questions about the vagus nerve and nutritional intake as a protective factor. Expect this topic to continue appearing in future exams. Key points to master: bidirectional communication, the vagus nerve's predominantly afferent role (80–90% gut-to-brain), and the evidence that gut microbiota influence neurotransmitter production and stress reactivity.

# 4. Selye's General Adaptation Syndrome (GAS)

## 4.1 The Model

Hans Selye (1907–1982), often called the "father of stress research," developed the **General Adaptation Syndrome (GAS)** as a **three-stage biological model** of the body's response to stress. His key insight, drawn from research at McGill University in the 1930s, was that the body responds to **all stressors** with the **same general physiological pattern**, regardless of the specific nature of the stressor. He called this the **non-specificity principle** — whether the stressor is physical (extreme cold, injury) or psychological (exam pressure, relationship conflict), the body's biological response follows the same three-stage sequence.

Selye's original 1936 research involved exposing rats to a range of noxious stimuli (cold, surgical injury, excessive exercise, toxic injections). Despite the different stressors, all rats developed the same triad of physiological changes: **enlarged adrenal cortex**, **shrunken thymus gland and lymph nodes** (immune suppression), and **stomach ulcers**.

### Stage 1: Alarm Reaction

The alarm reaction is the body's initial response to the stressor. It has two sub-phases:

**Shock phase:** The body's first awareness of the stressor. Resistance **drops below normal** as the body is caught unprepared. Blood pressure and body temperature fall temporarily. There may be a brief loss of muscle tone. This phase is typically very brief.

**Counter-shock phase:** The body mobilises its defences. The **sympathetic nervous system** activates, triggering the fight-flight-freeze response. Adrenaline is released from the adrenal medulla. Resistance **rises rapidly above normal** as the body prepares to deal with the threat. Heart rate, blood pressure, and breathing rate increase. Blood sugar is mobilised for energy.

### Stage 2: Resistance

If the stressor persists beyond the initial alarm, the body enters the resistance stage. The intense arousal of the alarm stage diminishes, but the body maintains a **sustained level of physiological arousal above normal**. The adrenal cortex releases **cortisol** to maintain energy supplies and repair minor damage. Non-essential physiological processes (digestion, growth, reproduction, immune function) are suppressed to conserve resources for coping with the stressor.

During the resistance stage, the person may appear to be coping well — they are functioning and adapting to the stressor. However, the body's resources are being gradually depleted. If the stressor is removed during this stage, the body returns to homeostasis (normal functioning). If the stressor continues, the body moves to the third stage.

### Stage 3: Exhaustion

If the stressor persists long enough, the body's resources become **depleted**. Resistance **falls below normal** — even below the initial pre-stress baseline. The person becomes highly vulnerable to:

- **Physical disorders:** hypertension, cardiovascular disease, stomach ulcers, chronic fatigue
- **Immune suppression:** increased susceptibility to infections and illness
- **Psychological disorders:** anxiety, depression, burnout
- In Selye's original animal experiments, prolonged exposure ultimately resulted in **death**

### The Standard GAS Graph

You should be able to draw and interpret the standard GAS graph from memory:

- **X-axis:** Time
- **Y-axis:** Level of Resistance to Stress
- **Horizontal dashed line:** Normal resistance level

The curve shows the characteristic "dip-rise-plateau-fall" shape:

1. **Shock** — resistance dips below normal (brief)
2. **Counter-shock** — resistance rises sharply above normal
3. **Resistance** — resistance plateaus above normal (sustained)
4. **Exhaustion** — resistance falls below normal and continues declining

## 4.2 Evaluating the Explanatory Power of GAS

The VCAA study design uses the phrase "explanatory power" three times across U3 AOS1, and the 2023 examiner report explicitly stated that students should practise evaluating the explanatory power of all stress models. **Evaluating** means making a judgement about how well the model explains a phenomenon — not just describing what the model says.

### Strengths of GAS

- **First model to systematically link chronic stress to disease.** Before Selye, the connection between prolonged stress and physical illness was not well understood. GAS provided a framework for understanding how stress "gets under the skin."
- **Rich physiological detail.** GAS describes specific biological mechanisms (adrenal activation, cortisol release, immune suppression) that can be measured and verified.
- **Strong laboratory evidence.** Selye's research demonstrated consistent, replicable physiological responses across different stressors and different organisms.
- **Explains non-specificity.** The model accounts for why very different stressors (physical injury, emotional trauma, illness) can produce similar physical outcomes (hypertension, ulcers, immune compromise).

### Limitations of GAS

- **Ignores individual differences.** GAS assumes everyone responds to stress in the same biological way, in the same sequence. In reality, people vary enormously in their stress responses based on personality, past experience, perceived control, and social support.
- **Ignores psychological factors.** GAS is a purely biological model — it does not account for cognitive appraisal, perception, or meaning-making. Two people facing the same stressor may experience very different stress responses depending on how they **interpret** the situation, but GAS cannot explain this.
- **Based primarily on animal research.** Selye's foundational research used rats, raising questions about the generalisability of findings to humans, who have more complex cognitive processes.
- **Assumes a linear progression.** GAS implies that everyone who faces chronic stress will progress through alarm → resistance → exhaustion in order. In practice, people may move back and forth between stages, or never reach exhaustion.
- **Does not explain eustress.** GAS treats all stress as leading toward negative outcomes (exhaustion, disease). It cannot account for positive stress responses (eustress) or situations where stress enhances performance.

> **⚠️ Exam Alert:** When the exam asks you to **evaluate** GAS, you must make a judgement — not just list strengths and limitations. A strong evaluation connects the model's features to the specific scenario in the question and explains **why** the model can or cannot adequately account for what is described. For example: "GAS has strong explanatory power for Mei's situation because her chronic work stress has persisted for months, and her symptoms (fatigue, frequent illness, high blood pressure) are consistent with the exhaustion stage, where prolonged cortisol exposure depletes bodily resources and suppresses immune function. However, GAS cannot explain why Mei experiences more severe stress than her colleague facing the same workload, because it does not account for individual differences in cognitive appraisal."

# 5. Lazarus and Folkman's Transactional Model of Stress and Coping

## 5.1 The Model

Richard Lazarus (1922–2002) and Susan Folkman (b. 1938) proposed that stress is not simply a stimulus or a response — it arises from the **transaction** (interaction) between an individual and their environment. The model's core principle is:

**Stress occurs when perceived demands exceed perceived resources to cope.**

Unlike GAS, which focuses on universal biological responses, the Transactional Model focuses on **cognitive appraisal** — the mental processes by which a person evaluates a situation and their ability to deal with it. This is why different people can experience very different levels of stress in response to the same stressor.

The model involves two stages of appraisal:

### Primary Appraisal

**Primary appraisal** is the person's initial evaluation of the **significance** of a situation — "What does this mean for me?" There are three possible outcomes:

- **Irrelevant:** The situation has no relevance to the person's wellbeing. No stress response occurs. (Example: hearing that a road you never use is closed for repairs.)
- **Benign-positive:** The situation is perceived as favourable or positive. No stress response occurs, and the person may experience positive emotions. (Example: receiving an unexpected compliment from a teacher.)
- **Stressful:** The situation is perceived as potentially harmful, threatening, or challenging. This triggers further evaluation. Stressful appraisals are further categorised as:
  - **Harm/loss:** Damage has already occurred. (Example: you have already failed an exam.)
  - **Threat:** There is potential for future harm or loss. (Example: you are about to sit a difficult exam you feel underprepared for.)
  - **Challenge:** There is potential for growth, gain, or mastery. (Example: you see the exam as an opportunity to demonstrate your knowledge.)

### Secondary Appraisal

**Secondary appraisal** is the person's evaluation of their **coping resources and options** — "What can I do about this?" The person assesses both:

- **Internal resources:** Confidence, knowledge, skills, past experience, physical fitness, emotional resilience
- **External resources:** Money, time, social support, access to information or professional help

If the person judges that their resources are **sufficient** to meet the demands, a stress response may be minimal or absent, and the person selects an appropriate coping strategy. If the person judges that the demands **greatly exceed** their resources, a significant stress response occurs.

> **⚠️ Exam Alert — Scope Limitation:** The VCAA study design specifies "primary and secondary appraisal only." **Reappraisal** (a third step where the person re-evaluates the situation after initial coping attempts) is NOT required by the study design. Students who include reappraisal are wasting time and word count on non-examinable content. Stick to primary and secondary appraisal only.

### Standard Flowchart

You should be able to draw this from memory:

```
Stressor → PRIMARY APPRAISAL
             ├── Irrelevant → No stress
             ├── Benign-positive → No stress
             └── Stressful
                  ├── Harm/loss
                  ├── Threat        → SECONDARY APPRAISAL
                  └── Challenge       ├── Resources sufficient → Coping strategy
                                      └── Resources insufficient → STRESS RESPONSE
```

## 5.2 Evaluating the Explanatory Power of the Transactional Model

### Strengths

- **Explains individual differences.** This is the model's central strength. Because stress depends on subjective appraisal, the model can explain why two people facing the same stressor experience different levels of stress — one may appraise it as a challenge while the other appraises it as a threat.
- **Focuses on controllable factors.** Appraisal and coping resources can be modified through psychological interventions (e.g. cognitive behavioural therapy, stress management training). This gives the model practical therapeutic value.
- **Based on human research.** Unlike GAS, the Transactional Model was developed from research with human participants, enhancing its relevance to human stress experiences.
- **Led to effective interventions.** The model underpins many evidence-based stress management approaches, including cognitive restructuring (changing how a person appraises a situation) and resource-building (developing coping skills and social support).

### Limitations

- **Cannot explain stress that occurs without conscious appraisal.** This is the model's most critical limitation. Some stress responses occur **automatically**, without any cognitive evaluation. Examples include: heart-rate increases during nightmares (the person is asleep and unable to consciously appraise), startle responses to sudden loud noises, and physiological stress responses in infants (who lack the cognitive capacity for appraisal).
- **Overlooks physiological responses.** The model focuses on psychological processes (appraisal, perception) and does not adequately explain the biological mechanisms of stress (cortisol release, immune suppression, autonomic activation).
- **Difficult to test experimentally.** Appraisal is a subjective, internal process that cannot be directly observed or precisely measured. Research relies on self-report, which is vulnerable to inaccuracies.
- **Assumes sequential appraisal.** The model implies that primary appraisal occurs before secondary appraisal. In reality, these processes often occur simultaneously or in rapid alternation, making them difficult to distinguish.

> **⚠️ Exam Alert — The Nightmare Question:** In the 2023 exam, Q7b asked students to evaluate the Transactional Model's appropriateness for explaining heart-rate increases during nightmares. **74% of students scored zero.** The mistake was describing what primary and secondary appraisal would look like in a nightmare, rather than evaluating whether the model **could explain the phenomenon at all**. The correct approach: the Transactional Model has **poor explanatory power** for nightmares because (1) the person is **asleep** and therefore unable to engage in **conscious cognitive appraisal**, and (2) heart-rate changes are **physiological responses** controlled by the autonomic nervous system, which the model (as a psychological model) does not adequately address. **Evaluate** means judge — does this model work here, or doesn't it? Don't describe the model when asked to evaluate it.

# 6. Comparing GAS and the Transactional Model

Understanding the differences between these two models is essential for exam questions that ask you to compare, contrast, or evaluate them. The table below captures the key distinctions:

| Feature | GAS (Selye) | Transactional Model (Lazarus & Folkman) |
|---------|-------------|----------------------------------------|
| **Focus** | Universal biological response | Individual cognitive appraisal |
| **Type of model** | Biological | Psychological |
| **Language** | Biological: cortisol, adrenaline, alarm, resistance, exhaustion, adrenal cortex, immune suppression | Psychological: appraisal, perceived threat/challenge/harm, coping resources, perceived demands |
| **Individual differences** | Cannot explain — assumes the same response for everyone | Central feature — explains why people respond differently to the same stressor |
| **Evidence base** | Primarily animal research (rats) | Primarily human research |
| **Strengths** | Explains physiology of chronic stress; links stress to disease | Explains individual variation; identifies controllable factors |
| **Limitations** | Ignores psychology and individual differences | Cannot explain stress without conscious appraisal; ignores physiology |
| **Best at explaining** | Chronic stress leading to physical illness; physiological stress responses | Individual variation in stress responses; why perception matters |
| **Poor at explaining** | Why two people respond differently to the same stressor | Automatic physiological responses; stress in sleep, infants, or unconscious states |

> **⚠️ Exam Alert — Match Your Language to the Model:** The examiners penalise students who mix biological language with the psychological model or vice versa. When discussing GAS, use biological terms: cortisol, adrenaline, adrenal cortex, adrenal medulla, alarm/resistance/exhaustion, sympathetic activation, immune suppression. When discussing the Transactional Model, use psychological terms: appraisal, perceived threat/challenge/harm, coping resources, perceived demands. In the 2023 exam, only 38% of students correctly answered a multiple-choice question because the distractors used Lazarus & Folkman language in options about GAS. If you can keep the vocabularies separate, you have a significant advantage.

# 7. A Framework for Evaluating Explanatory Power

The VCAA expects you to be able to evaluate the explanatory power of GAS, the Transactional Model, and the fight-flight-freeze response across **diverse scenarios** — not just the familiar textbook examples. The 2023 examiner report explicitly recommended that students practise this skill throughout the year.

Here is a simple four-question framework you can apply to any scenario:

1. **Can this model explain what is happening in this scenario?**
2. **What can it explain well?** (Which specific features of the scenario does it account for?)
3. **What can't it explain?** (Which features of the scenario fall outside the model's scope?)
4. **Why?** (What is it about the model's focus — biological vs psychological — that creates this limitation?)

### Practice: Applying the Framework

**Scenario A: Workplace Burnout**
*Mei has been working 60-hour weeks for eight months. She is exhausted, frequently sick, and has developed high blood pressure.*

- **GAS:** Strong explanatory power. Mei's symptoms align with the exhaustion stage — prolonged stress has depleted her resources, leading to immune suppression (frequent illness), cardiovascular effects (high blood pressure), and fatigue. GAS's non-specificity principle explains why chronic work stress produces physical symptoms.
- **Transactional Model:** Moderate explanatory power. It can explain why Mei experiences stress (she may appraise her workload as exceeding her resources), but it cannot explain the specific physiological symptoms. It could explain why a colleague in the same role might cope better (different appraisal of demands vs resources), but not Mei's high blood pressure or immune suppression.

**Scenario B: Nightmares**
*Twelve-year-old Zac experiences increased heart rate and sweating during a nightmare about being chased.*

- **GAS:** Moderate explanatory power. The alarm reaction's activation of the sympathetic NS (increased heart rate, sweating) matches Zac's symptoms. However, GAS does not distinguish between conscious and unconscious experiences of stress, and it is unclear whether the full three-stage model applies to a brief nightmare.
- **Transactional Model:** Poor explanatory power. Zac is asleep and unable to engage in conscious cognitive appraisal. His heart-rate increase and sweating are involuntary physiological responses, not products of deliberate evaluation of demands versus resources. The model fundamentally requires conscious appraisal, which cannot occur during sleep.
- **Fight-flight-freeze:** Strong explanatory power. The amygdala can activate the sympathetic NS during a nightmare based on dream content, triggering fight-or-flight responses (increased heart rate, sweating) without conscious appraisal.

**Scenario C: Exam Preparation**
*Two students, Aisha and Ben, are preparing for the same VCE Psychology exam. Aisha feels motivated and focused; Ben feels overwhelmed and anxious.*

- **GAS:** Poor explanatory power. Both students face the same stressor, but their responses are completely different — one experiences eustress, the other distress. GAS predicts the same biological response for both and cannot account for this individual variation.
- **Transactional Model:** Strong explanatory power. Aisha may appraise the exam as a **challenge** (potential for mastery) and evaluate her resources as sufficient (good preparation, strong study skills). Ben may appraise it as a **threat** (potential for failure) and evaluate his resources as insufficient (feeling underprepared, lacking confidence). The difference in their stress responses is entirely explained by differences in appraisal.

# 8. Coping Strategies

## 8.1 Approach and Avoidance Strategies

**Approach coping strategies** involve directly confronting or addressing the stressor. Examples include:

- Seeking expert advice or information about the problem
- Creating a structured action plan
- Problem-solving and brainstorming solutions
- Seeking social support (talking to friends, family, or professionals)
- Directly addressing a conflict through open communication
- Developing new skills relevant to the challenge

Approach strategies are generally associated with **better long-term mental health outcomes**. People who habitually use approach coping report fewer psychological symptoms and greater perceived control over their lives.

**Avoidance coping strategies** involve evading, minimising, or disengaging from the stressor. Examples include:

- Denial (refusing to acknowledge the problem exists)
- Distraction (turning attention to unrelated activities)
- Procrastination (delaying action on the stressor)
- Fantasy or wishful thinking
- Substance use (alcohol, drugs)
- Emotional disengagement (suppressing feelings about the stressor)

> **⚠️ Exam Alert:** A common misconception — and a frequent source of lost marks — is that avoidance coping is always maladaptive. This is **not true**. Avoidance strategies can be **appropriate and effective** when the stressor is genuinely **uncontrollable**. For example, while waiting for medical test results (a stressor you cannot influence), distraction is a reasonable and healthy coping strategy. Dwelling on the results would increase anxiety without changing the outcome. The key principle is **context-specific effectiveness** (Section 8.2).

## 8.2 Context-Specific Effectiveness

**Context-specific effectiveness** means that a coping strategy's appropriateness depends on the **match between the strategy and the situation**. No strategy is universally good or universally bad — effectiveness depends on context.

The general principle is:

- **Approach strategies** are generally more effective when the stressor is **controllable** — when the person can actually do something to address or change the situation (e.g. studying for an exam, seeking treatment for a health condition, having a difficult conversation to resolve a conflict).
- **Avoidance strategies** are sometimes more appropriate when the stressor is **uncontrollable** — when the person cannot change the situation and must endure it (e.g. waiting for surgery, grieving the death of a loved one, experiencing an unavoidable natural disaster). In these situations, approach strategies may increase frustration without reducing stress.

However, even when avoidance is initially appropriate, **excessive long-term reliance** on avoidance strategies is associated with poorer mental health outcomes. A person who uses avoidance to cope with every stressor — including controllable ones — is likely to experience increased anxiety, unresolved problems, and a diminished sense of control.

## 8.3 Coping Flexibility

**Coping flexibility** is the ability to **modify, adjust, or switch coping strategies based on changing situational demands**. It involves three components:

1. **Awareness:** Recognising that the current strategy is ineffective or no longer appropriate
2. **Discontinuation:** Stopping the ineffective strategy
3. **Replacement:** Implementing a different, more suitable strategy

High coping flexibility is associated with **better mental health outcomes** — people who can adapt their coping approach to match changing circumstances experience less distress and recover more quickly from stressful events (Kato, 2012). Low coping flexibility — rigidly persisting with the same coping strategy regardless of whether it is working — is associated with depressive symptoms and prolonged distress.

**Example:** A student initially copes with exam stress by creating a detailed study schedule (approach strategy). Midway through the exam period, she realises she is burning out and switches to incorporating regular exercise and social activities (a blend of approach and avoidance). After the exams, she uses distraction and leisure activities to decompress (avoidance strategy). Her ability to recognise when each strategy is working, stop when it isn't, and try something different demonstrates high coping flexibility.

> **⚠️ Exam Alert — The 2024 Coping Flexibility Disaster:** In 2024, Q5c asked about the relationship between coping flexibility and mindfulness meditation. Only **1% of students scored full marks**, and 43% scored zero. The key insight students missed: **mindfulness meditation emphasises sustained, non-judgemental acceptance of present-moment experience** — it inherently involves committing to a single, consistent approach (acceptance) rather than switching strategies. This makes it **potentially incompatible** with coping flexibility, which requires the ability to abandon an approach and try something different. High-scoring responses explained this tension: mindfulness can be one valuable tool within a flexible coping repertoire, but mindfulness *as a sole strategy* limits flexibility. If a question asks you to evaluate coping flexibility in relation to another concept, think about whether the other concept **supports or constrains** the ability to switch strategies.

# 9. Bringing It All Together: Stress as a Psychobiological Process

The word "psychobiological" in the heading of this Area of Study is not decorative — it tells you exactly how the VCAA wants you to think about stress. Stress involves both **psychological** processes (perception, appraisal, emotional responses) and **biological** processes (sympathetic activation, cortisol release, neurotransmitter changes, gut-brain communication). Neither dimension alone captures the full picture.

This is why the study design includes **both** a biological model (GAS) and a psychological model (the Transactional Model). Each captures a different aspect of the stress experience. GAS explains the body's universal physiological response to chronic stress. The Transactional Model explains why individuals differ in their stress responses based on cognitive appraisal. The fight-flight-freeze response explains immediate, acute threat responses. The gut-brain axis reveals a newly understood pathway through which stress affects — and is affected by — the body's largest collection of microorganisms.

For the exam, the highest-scoring responses will be those that can move fluently between biological and psychological explanations, selecting the model or mechanism most appropriate to the specific scenario, and acknowledging the limitations of each. This is the essence of a psychobiological approach — understanding that mind and body are not separate systems but deeply interconnected ones.

# Why It Matters

Stress is one of the most universally experienced psychological phenomena. Every student sitting the VCE Psychology exam has experienced stress — possibly about that very exam. But stress is far more than a feeling. It is a complex **psychobiological process** — meaning it involves both psychological and biological mechanisms working together. Your body's stress response evolved to keep you alive in genuinely dangerous situations. The problem is that the same system that helped your ancestors escape predators now activates when you receive a difficult email, sit in traffic, or think about an upcoming deadline.

This chapter connects directly to everything you learned in Chapter 1. The nervous system structures you studied — the sympathetic and parasympathetic divisions, neurotransmitters, the enteric nervous system — are the very systems that produce and regulate the stress response. Stress is, in effect, the nervous system in action under pressure.

This is also where the exam gets hard. The 2023 and 2024 exams both tested stress content extensively, and student performance was notably poor on questions requiring **evaluation** of stress models and **application** to novel scenarios. In 2023, 74% of students scored zero when asked to evaluate the Transactional Model's ability to explain heart-rate changes during nightmares. In 2024, only 1% of students scored full marks on a question about coping flexibility and mindfulness meditation. These are not content failures — they are application failures. Students knew the models but could not use them to reason about unfamiliar situations. This chapter will teach you not only what the models say, but how to evaluate what they can and cannot explain.

# 1. Understanding Stress: Stressors and Stress Responses

## 1.1 Defining Stress

**Stress** is a state of physiological and psychological arousal produced by internal or external stressors that are perceived as challenging or exceeding a person's resources to cope. Notice that this definition has both a biological component (physiological arousal) and a psychological component (perception of challenge) — this is why stress is described as a **psychobiological process**.

A **stressor** is any stimulus — internal or external — that causes a stress response. Stressors are not inherently positive or negative; what matters is how the individual perceives and responds to them.

**Eustress** is a **positive** stress response characterised by feelings of motivation, excitement, alertness, and focused energy. It occurs when a person perceives a stressor as manageable and potentially beneficial. Examples include the nervous excitement before a sporting final, the anticipation of starting a new job, or the energising pressure of a tight deadline that motivates productive work.

**Distress** is a **negative** stress response characterised by anxiety, tension, anger, helplessness, or feeling overwhelmed. It occurs when a person perceives a stressor as threatening, harmful, or exceeding their capacity to cope. Examples include dread before a difficult exam, grief after a relationship breakdown, or chronic anxiety about financial pressures.

> **⚠️ Exam Alert:** Eustress and distress are types of **responses**, not types of stressors. The same stressor can produce eustress in one person and distress in another, depending on their perception and resources. A job interview might produce eustress in a well-prepared, confident candidate and distress in someone who feels underprepared. If a question asks you to distinguish eustress from distress, focus on the **response** (how the person feels and functions), not on the stressor itself.

## 1.2 Internal and External Stressors

**Internal stressors** originate **within the individual**. They include negative self-talk ("I'm going to fail"), perfectionism, illness, chronic pain, fatigue, hormonal changes (such as those during puberty or menstruation), and unrealistic personal expectations.

**External stressors** originate from the **environment**. They can be categorised by intensity:

- **Daily pressures (hassles):** traffic, homework deadlines, minor disagreements, running late
- **Life events:** moving house, starting a new school, getting married, having a baby
- **Acculturative stress:** challenges of adapting to a new culture, including language barriers, discrimination, and loss of familiar social support
- **Major stressors:** serious illness or injury, violent crime, death of a loved one, divorce
- **Catastrophes:** natural disasters (such as the 2019–20 Black Summer bushfires, the 2022 Victorian floods), pandemics, war

## 1.3 Physiological and Psychological Stress Responses

Stress produces responses across three dimensions:

**Physiological responses** are the body's automatic biological reactions: increased heart rate and blood pressure, rapid breathing, sweating, muscle tension, pupil dilation, suppressed digestion, and elevated cortisol and adrenaline levels. These are mediated primarily by the sympathetic nervous system and the HPA axis (covered in Section 2).

**Psychological responses** encompass changes in cognition, emotion, and behaviour:

- *Cognitive effects:* difficulty concentrating, racing thoughts, indecisiveness, mental "blanking," impaired memory
- *Emotional effects:* anxiety, irritability, anger, sadness, helplessness, fear
- *Behavioural effects:* withdrawal from social activities, changes in eating or sleeping patterns, substance use, aggression, procrastination

# 2. The Fight-Flight-Freeze Response and the Biology of Stress

## 2.1 The Fight-Flight-Freeze Response

The **fight-flight-freeze response** is the body's involuntary, immediate reaction to a perceived acute threat. It is controlled by the autonomic nervous system and occurs without conscious decision-making.

The process begins when the **amygdala** (a small, almond-shaped structure deep in the brain's temporal lobe that processes emotions, especially fear) detects danger — either from direct sensory input or from the brain's interpretation of a situation. The amygdala signals the **hypothalamus**, which acts as the command centre, activating the **sympathetic nervous system**.

### Fight or Flight

When the sympathetic nervous system becomes dominant, the **adrenal medulla** (the inner part of the adrenal gland, sitting on top of each kidney) releases **adrenaline** (also called epinephrine) and **noradrenaline** (norepinephrine) into the bloodstream. These hormones produce rapid physiological changes designed to prepare the body for vigorous physical action:

- Heart rate and blood pressure increase (to pump more blood to muscles)
- Breathing rate increases and bronchioles dilate (to take in more oxygen)
- Blood is redistributed from the digestive system to skeletal muscles (to fuel movement)
- Pupils dilate (to improve visual acuity)
- Sweating increases (to cool the body during exertion)
- Blood glucose levels rise (to provide energy)
- Digestion is suppressed (energy is redirected to more immediately useful functions)

These changes prepare the body to either confront the threat (**fight**) or escape from it (**flight**).

### Freeze

The **freeze response** occurs when the threat seems overwhelming, inescapable, or when the person cannot determine whether to fight or flee. Contrary to what many students assume, freeze is **not simply more sympathetic activation**. Instead, the **parasympathetic nervous system overrides the sympathetic nervous system**, producing:

- Body movements and vocalisations stop (tonic immobility)
- Heart rate decelerates
- Blood pressure drops
- Muscles may collapse or become rigid
- The person may feel "frozen," unable to move or speak

The freeze response has evolutionary value: in predator-prey situations, remaining completely still can avoid detection. In modern contexts, it is commonly experienced during traumatic events, sudden shock, or overwhelming fear.

> **⚠️ Exam Alert:** The 2023 examiner report noted that many students incorrectly identify the sympathetic NS as dominant during freeze. This is wrong. The **sympathetic NS** is dominant during **fight and flight**. The **parasympathetic NS** is dominant during **freeze**. If a question asks which division is dominant during the freeze response, the answer is parasympathetic.

## 2.2 Cortisol and the Chronic Stress Response

The fight-flight-freeze response is designed for **acute** (short-term) threats. But what happens when the stressor doesn't go away? When stress becomes chronic, a different biological system takes over: the **HPA axis**.

The **HPA axis** (Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Adrenal axis) is a slower, sustained hormonal stress response pathway:

1. The **hypothalamus** releases corticotropin-releasing hormone (**CRH**)
2. CRH stimulates the **anterior pituitary gland** to release adrenocorticotropic hormone (**ACTH**)
3. ACTH travels via the bloodstream to the **adrenal cortex** (the outer layer of the adrenal gland — distinct from the adrenal medulla)
4. The adrenal cortex releases **cortisol**

**Cortisol** is the body's primary chronic stress hormone. Its functions include increasing blood sugar levels, enhancing the metabolism of fats and proteins for energy, suppressing the immune system, and retarding tissue repair. In the short term, these effects are adaptive — they keep the body fuelled and functioning under pressure.

However, **prolonged cortisol exposure** (from chronic stress) has serious harmful effects:

- Hypertension and cardiovascular disease
- **Hippocampal shrinkage** — impairing memory formation and retrieval
- Weakened immune function — increasing vulnerability to illness
- Disrupted sleep patterns
- Increased vulnerability to anxiety and depression
- Weight gain (particularly abdominal fat)

> **⚠️ Exam Alert — Critical Distinction:** Students frequently confuse adrenaline and cortisol. Remember: **adrenaline** comes from the adrenal **medulla** and is released during **acute** stress (immediate, short-term). **Cortisol** comes from the adrenal **cortex** and is released during **chronic** stress (sustained, long-term). Both can operate simultaneously, but they serve different temporal functions and come from different parts of the adrenal gland. In 2024, students confused cortisol depletion with adrenaline depletion when answering questions about the exhaustion stage of GAS. Use the correct hormone for the correct context.

# 3. The Gut-Brain Axis (GBA)

## 3.1 What Is the Gut-Brain Axis?

The **gut-brain axis (GBA)** is a **bidirectional communication link** between the brain and the gastrointestinal (GI) tract. "Bidirectional" means that signals travel in both directions — the brain influences the gut, and the gut influences the brain.

The VCAA study design includes the GBA as "an area of emerging research" — this means you need to understand the basic principles, know its significance for stress and the nervous system, and recognise that research in this area is still developing. You do not need to have memorised every molecular pathway, but you do need to understand the key mechanisms and be able to discuss them in relation to stress.

The GBA communicates through multiple pathways:

**The vagus nerve** is the primary neural link between the gut and brain. It is the longest cranial nerve in the body, running from the brainstem to the abdomen. Approximately **80–90% of vagus nerve fibres are afferent** (carrying information from the gut to the brain), while only **10–20% are efferent** (carrying information from the brain to the gut). This means the gut sends far more information to the brain than the brain sends to the gut.

**The HPA axis** provides a hormonal link — cortisol released during stress alters the composition of gut microbiota, which in turn affects gut function and can feed back to influence brain function.

**The immune system** provides another link — approximately 70% of the body's immune tissue is gut-associated. Gut inflammation can trigger immune signals that affect brain function, mood, and behaviour.

**Microbial metabolites** are chemicals produced by gut bacteria that influence brain function. Gut microbiota produce neurotransmitters including approximately **95% of the body's serotonin**, as well as GABA and dopamine. These chemicals influence mood, anxiety, sleep, and cognitive function.

## 3.2 Gut Microbiota and Stress

**Gut microbiota** refers to the trillions of microorganisms (bacteria, fungi, viruses) living in the gastrointestinal tract. A **diverse and balanced** gut microbiota is associated with better mental wellbeing and reduced stress reactivity. **Dysbiosis** — an imbalance in gut microbiota composition — is linked to increased stress, anxiety, depression, and gastrointestinal disorders such as irritable bowel syndrome (IBS).

The relationship between gut microbiota and stress is bidirectional:

- **Stress affects the gut:** Chronic stress and elevated cortisol alter the composition of gut microbiota, reducing diversity and promoting the growth of harmful bacteria. This can lead to digestive problems, inflammation, and further disruption of normal gut function.
- **The gut affects stress:** Changes in gut microbiota composition alter the production of neurotransmitters and immune signals that travel to the brain, influencing mood, anxiety, and stress reactivity. An unhealthy gut can amplify the stress response; a healthy gut can dampen it.

### Research in Focus: Key GBA Studies

**Sudo et al. (2004):** Researchers raised mice in completely germ-free environments (no gut bacteria at all). These germ-free mice showed an **exaggerated HPA stress response** — they produced significantly more cortisol when exposed to stress compared to normal mice. When the germ-free mice were then colonised with *Bifidobacterium infantis* (a beneficial bacterium), their stress response **normalised**. This demonstrated that gut microbiota is essential for normal stress regulation.

**Bravo et al. (2011):** Mice fed the probiotic *Lactobacillus rhamnosus* showed altered GABA receptor expression in the brain and **reduced stress-related behaviour** (less anxiety, less depressive-like behaviour). Crucially, these effects **disappeared entirely when the vagus nerve was severed** — confirming that the vagus nerve is the key communication pathway through which gut bacteria influence brain function.

> **⚠️ Exam Alert:** The gut-brain axis is a flagship topic of the 2023–2027 study design — it was brand new when this study design was introduced. In 2023, the GBA was the focus of the **10-mark extended response** (the highest-value question on the exam), asking students to analyse a study on psychobiotic diets and discuss the role of gut microbiota in stress. In 2024, GBA content appeared in short-answer questions about the vagus nerve and nutritional intake as a protective factor. Expect this topic to continue appearing in future exams. Key points to master: bidirectional communication, the vagus nerve's predominantly afferent role (80–90% gut-to-brain), and the evidence that gut microbiota influence neurotransmitter production and stress reactivity.

# 4. Selye's General Adaptation Syndrome (GAS)

## 4.1 The Model

Hans Selye (1907–1982), often called the "father of stress research," developed the **General Adaptation Syndrome (GAS)** as a **three-stage biological model** of the body's response to stress. His key insight, drawn from research at McGill University in the 1930s, was that the body responds to **all stressors** with the **same general physiological pattern**, regardless of the specific nature of the stressor. He called this the **non-specificity principle** — whether the stressor is physical (extreme cold, injury) or psychological (exam pressure, relationship conflict), the body's biological response follows the same three-stage sequence.

Selye's original 1936 research involved exposing rats to a range of noxious stimuli (cold, surgical injury, excessive exercise, toxic injections). Despite the different stressors, all rats developed the same triad of physiological changes: **enlarged adrenal cortex**, **shrunken thymus gland and lymph nodes** (immune suppression), and **stomach ulcers**.

### Stage 1: Alarm Reaction

The alarm reaction is the body's initial response to the stressor. It has two sub-phases:

**Shock phase:** The body's first awareness of the stressor. Resistance **drops below normal** as the body is caught unprepared. Blood pressure and body temperature fall temporarily. There may be a brief loss of muscle tone. This phase is typically very brief.

**Counter-shock phase:** The body mobilises its defences. The **sympathetic nervous system** activates, triggering the fight-flight-freeze response. Adrenaline is released from the adrenal medulla. Resistance **rises rapidly above normal** as the body prepares to deal with the threat. Heart rate, blood pressure, and breathing rate increase. Blood sugar is mobilised for energy.

### Stage 2: Resistance

If the stressor persists beyond the initial alarm, the body enters the resistance stage. The intense arousal of the alarm stage diminishes, but the body maintains a **sustained level of physiological arousal above normal**. The adrenal cortex releases **cortisol** to maintain energy supplies and repair minor damage. Non-essential physiological processes (digestion, growth, reproduction, immune function) are suppressed to conserve resources for coping with the stressor.

During the resistance stage, the person may appear to be coping well — they are functioning and adapting to the stressor. However, the body's resources are being gradually depleted. If the stressor is removed during this stage, the body returns to homeostasis (normal functioning). If the stressor continues, the body moves to the third stage.

### Stage 3: Exhaustion

If the stressor persists long enough, the body's resources become **depleted**. Resistance **falls below normal** — even below the initial pre-stress baseline. The person becomes highly vulnerable to:

- **Physical disorders:** hypertension, cardiovascular disease, stomach ulcers, chronic fatigue
- **Immune suppression:** increased susceptibility to infections and illness
- **Psychological disorders:** anxiety, depression, burnout
- In Selye's original animal experiments, prolonged exposure ultimately resulted in **death**

### The Standard GAS Graph

You should be able to draw and interpret the standard GAS graph from memory:

- **X-axis:** Time
- **Y-axis:** Level of Resistance to Stress
- **Horizontal dashed line:** Normal resistance level

The curve shows the characteristic "dip-rise-plateau-fall" shape:

1. **Shock** — resistance dips below normal (brief)
2. **Counter-shock** — resistance rises sharply above normal
3. **Resistance** — resistance plateaus above normal (sustained)
4. **Exhaustion** — resistance falls below normal and continues declining

## 4.2 Evaluating the Explanatory Power of GAS

The VCAA study design uses the phrase "explanatory power" three times across U3 AOS1, and the 2023 examiner report explicitly stated that students should practise evaluating the explanatory power of all stress models. **Evaluating** means making a judgement about how well the model explains a phenomenon — not just describing what the model says.

### Strengths of GAS

- **First model to systematically link chronic stress to disease.** Before Selye, the connection between prolonged stress and physical illness was not well understood. GAS provided a framework for understanding how stress "gets under the skin."
- **Rich physiological detail.** GAS describes specific biological mechanisms (adrenal activation, cortisol release, immune suppression) that can be measured and verified.
- **Strong laboratory evidence.** Selye's research demonstrated consistent, replicable physiological responses across different stressors and different organisms.
- **Explains non-specificity.** The model accounts for why very different stressors (physical injury, emotional trauma, illness) can produce similar physical outcomes (hypertension, ulcers, immune compromise).

### Limitations of GAS

- **Ignores individual differences.** GAS assumes everyone responds to stress in the same biological way, in the same sequence. In reality, people vary enormously in their stress responses based on personality, past experience, perceived control, and social support.
- **Ignores psychological factors.** GAS is a purely biological model — it does not account for cognitive appraisal, perception, or meaning-making. Two people facing the same stressor may experience very different stress responses depending on how they **interpret** the situation, but GAS cannot explain this.
- **Based primarily on animal research.** Selye's foundational research used rats, raising questions about the generalisability of findings to humans, who have more complex cognitive processes.
- **Assumes a linear progression.** GAS implies that everyone who faces chronic stress will progress through alarm → resistance → exhaustion in order. In practice, people may move back and forth between stages, or never reach exhaustion.
- **Does not explain eustress.** GAS treats all stress as leading toward negative outcomes (exhaustion, disease). It cannot account for positive stress responses (eustress) or situations where stress enhances performance.

> **⚠️ Exam Alert:** When the exam asks you to **evaluate** GAS, you must make a judgement — not just list strengths and limitations. A strong evaluation connects the model's features to the specific scenario in the question and explains **why** the model can or cannot adequately account for what is described. For example: "GAS has strong explanatory power for Mei's situation because her chronic work stress has persisted for months, and her symptoms (fatigue, frequent illness, high blood pressure) are consistent with the exhaustion stage, where prolonged cortisol exposure depletes bodily resources and suppresses immune function. However, GAS cannot explain why Mei experiences more severe stress than her colleague facing the same workload, because it does not account for individual differences in cognitive appraisal."

# 5. Lazarus and Folkman's Transactional Model of Stress and Coping

## 5.1 The Model

Richard Lazarus (1922–2002) and Susan Folkman (b. 1938) proposed that stress is not simply a stimulus or a response — it arises from the **transaction** (interaction) between an individual and their environment. The model's core principle is:

**Stress occurs when perceived demands exceed perceived resources to cope.**

Unlike GAS, which focuses on universal biological responses, the Transactional Model focuses on **cognitive appraisal** — the mental processes by which a person evaluates a situation and their ability to deal with it. This is why different people can experience very different levels of stress in response to the same stressor.

The model involves two stages of appraisal:

### Primary Appraisal

**Primary appraisal** is the person's initial evaluation of the **significance** of a situation — "What does this mean for me?" There are three possible outcomes:

- **Irrelevant:** The situation has no relevance to the person's wellbeing. No stress response occurs. (Example: hearing that a road you never use is closed for repairs.)
- **Benign-positive:** The situation is perceived as favourable or positive. No stress response occurs, and the person may experience positive emotions. (Example: receiving an unexpected compliment from a teacher.)
- **Stressful:** The situation is perceived as potentially harmful, threatening, or challenging. This triggers further evaluation. Stressful appraisals are further categorised as:
  - **Harm/loss:** Damage has already occurred. (Example: you have already failed an exam.)
  - **Threat:** There is potential for future harm or loss. (Example: you are about to sit a difficult exam you feel underprepared for.)
  - **Challenge:** There is potential for growth, gain, or mastery. (Example: you see the exam as an opportunity to demonstrate your knowledge.)

### Secondary Appraisal

**Secondary appraisal** is the person's evaluation of their **coping resources and options** — "What can I do about this?" The person assesses both:

- **Internal resources:** Confidence, knowledge, skills, past experience, physical fitness, emotional resilience
- **External resources:** Money, time, social support, access to information or professional help

If the person judges that their resources are **sufficient** to meet the demands, a stress response may be minimal or absent, and the person selects an appropriate coping strategy. If the person judges that the demands **greatly exceed** their resources, a significant stress response occurs.

> **⚠️ Exam Alert — Scope Limitation:** The VCAA study design specifies "primary and secondary appraisal only." **Reappraisal** (a third step where the person re-evaluates the situation after initial coping attempts) is NOT required by the study design. Students who include reappraisal are wasting time and word count on non-examinable content. Stick to primary and secondary appraisal only.

### Standard Flowchart

You should be able to draw this from memory:

```
Stressor → PRIMARY APPRAISAL
             ├── Irrelevant → No stress
             ├── Benign-positive → No stress
             └── Stressful
                  ├── Harm/loss
                  ├── Threat        → SECONDARY APPRAISAL
                  └── Challenge       ├── Resources sufficient → Coping strategy
                                      └── Resources insufficient → STRESS RESPONSE
```

## 5.2 Evaluating the Explanatory Power of the Transactional Model

### Strengths

- **Explains individual differences.** This is the model's central strength. Because stress depends on subjective appraisal, the model can explain why two people facing the same stressor experience different levels of stress — one may appraise it as a challenge while the other appraises it as a threat.
- **Focuses on controllable factors.** Appraisal and coping resources can be modified through psychological interventions (e.g. cognitive behavioural therapy, stress management training). This gives the model practical therapeutic value.
- **Based on human research.** Unlike GAS, the Transactional Model was developed from research with human participants, enhancing its relevance to human stress experiences.
- **Led to effective interventions.** The model underpins many evidence-based stress management approaches, including cognitive restructuring (changing how a person appraises a situation) and resource-building (developing coping skills and social support).

### Limitations

- **Cannot explain stress that occurs without conscious appraisal.** This is the model's most critical limitation. Some stress responses occur **automatically**, without any cognitive evaluation. Examples include: heart-rate increases during nightmares (the person is asleep and unable to consciously appraise), startle responses to sudden loud noises, and physiological stress responses in infants (who lack the cognitive capacity for appraisal).
- **Overlooks physiological responses.** The model focuses on psychological processes (appraisal, perception) and does not adequately explain the biological mechanisms of stress (cortisol release, immune suppression, autonomic activation).
- **Difficult to test experimentally.** Appraisal is a subjective, internal process that cannot be directly observed or precisely measured. Research relies on self-report, which is vulnerable to inaccuracies.
- **Assumes sequential appraisal.** The model implies that primary appraisal occurs before secondary appraisal. In reality, these processes often occur simultaneously or in rapid alternation, making them difficult to distinguish.

> **⚠️ Exam Alert — The Nightmare Question:** In the 2023 exam, Q7b asked students to evaluate the Transactional Model's appropriateness for explaining heart-rate increases during nightmares. **74% of students scored zero.** The mistake was describing what primary and secondary appraisal would look like in a nightmare, rather than evaluating whether the model **could explain the phenomenon at all**. The correct approach: the Transactional Model has **poor explanatory power** for nightmares because (1) the person is **asleep** and therefore unable to engage in **conscious cognitive appraisal**, and (2) heart-rate changes are **physiological responses** controlled by the autonomic nervous system, which the model (as a psychological model) does not adequately address. **Evaluate** means judge — does this model work here, or doesn't it? Don't describe the model when asked to evaluate it.

# 6. Comparing GAS and the Transactional Model

Understanding the differences between these two models is essential for exam questions that ask you to compare, contrast, or evaluate them. The table below captures the key distinctions:

| Feature | GAS (Selye) | Transactional Model (Lazarus & Folkman) |
|---------|-------------|----------------------------------------|
| **Focus** | Universal biological response | Individual cognitive appraisal |
| **Type of model** | Biological | Psychological |
| **Language** | Biological: cortisol, adrenaline, alarm, resistance, exhaustion, adrenal cortex, immune suppression | Psychological: appraisal, perceived threat/challenge/harm, coping resources, perceived demands |
| **Individual differences** | Cannot explain — assumes the same response for everyone | Central feature — explains why people respond differently to the same stressor |
| **Evidence base** | Primarily animal research (rats) | Primarily human research |
| **Strengths** | Explains physiology of chronic stress; links stress to disease | Explains individual variation; identifies controllable factors |
| **Limitations** | Ignores psychology and individual differences | Cannot explain stress without conscious appraisal; ignores physiology |
| **Best at explaining** | Chronic stress leading to physical illness; physiological stress responses | Individual variation in stress responses; why perception matters |
| **Poor at explaining** | Why two people respond differently to the same stressor | Automatic physiological responses; stress in sleep, infants, or unconscious states |

> **⚠️ Exam Alert — Match Your Language to the Model:** The examiners penalise students who mix biological language with the psychological model or vice versa. When discussing GAS, use biological terms: cortisol, adrenaline, adrenal cortex, adrenal medulla, alarm/resistance/exhaustion, sympathetic activation, immune suppression. When discussing the Transactional Model, use psychological terms: appraisal, perceived threat/challenge/harm, coping resources, perceived demands. In the 2023 exam, only 38% of students correctly answered a multiple-choice question because the distractors used Lazarus & Folkman language in options about GAS. If you can keep the vocabularies separate, you have a significant advantage.

# 7. A Framework for Evaluating Explanatory Power

The VCAA expects you to be able to evaluate the explanatory power of GAS, the Transactional Model, and the fight-flight-freeze response across **diverse scenarios** — not just the familiar textbook examples. The 2023 examiner report explicitly recommended that students practise this skill throughout the year.

Here is a simple four-question framework you can apply to any scenario:

1. **Can this model explain what is happening in this scenario?**
2. **What can it explain well?** (Which specific features of the scenario does it account for?)
3. **What can't it explain?** (Which features of the scenario fall outside the model's scope?)
4. **Why?** (What is it about the model's focus — biological vs psychological — that creates this limitation?)

### Practice: Applying the Framework

**Scenario A: Workplace Burnout**
*Mei has been working 60-hour weeks for eight months. She is exhausted, frequently sick, and has developed high blood pressure.*

- **GAS:** Strong explanatory power. Mei's symptoms align with the exhaustion stage — prolonged stress has depleted her resources, leading to immune suppression (frequent illness), cardiovascular effects (high blood pressure), and fatigue. GAS's non-specificity principle explains why chronic work stress produces physical symptoms.
- **Transactional Model:** Moderate explanatory power. It can explain why Mei experiences stress (she may appraise her workload as exceeding her resources), but it cannot explain the specific physiological symptoms. It could explain why a colleague in the same role might cope better (different appraisal of demands vs resources), but not Mei's high blood pressure or immune suppression.

**Scenario B: Nightmares**
*Twelve-year-old Zac experiences increased heart rate and sweating during a nightmare about being chased.*

- **GAS:** Moderate explanatory power. The alarm reaction's activation of the sympathetic NS (increased heart rate, sweating) matches Zac's symptoms. However, GAS does not distinguish between conscious and unconscious experiences of stress, and it is unclear whether the full three-stage model applies to a brief nightmare.
- **Transactional Model:** Poor explanatory power. Zac is asleep and unable to engage in conscious cognitive appraisal. His heart-rate increase and sweating are involuntary physiological responses, not products of deliberate evaluation of demands versus resources. The model fundamentally requires conscious appraisal, which cannot occur during sleep.
- **Fight-flight-freeze:** Strong explanatory power. The amygdala can activate the sympathetic NS during a nightmare based on dream content, triggering fight-or-flight responses (increased heart rate, sweating) without conscious appraisal.

**Scenario C: Exam Preparation**
*Two students, Aisha and Ben, are preparing for the same VCE Psychology exam. Aisha feels motivated and focused; Ben feels overwhelmed and anxious.*

- **GAS:** Poor explanatory power. Both students face the same stressor, but their responses are completely different — one experiences eustress, the other distress. GAS predicts the same biological response for both and cannot account for this individual variation.
- **Transactional Model:** Strong explanatory power. Aisha may appraise the exam as a **challenge** (potential for mastery) and evaluate her resources as sufficient (good preparation, strong study skills). Ben may appraise it as a **threat** (potential for failure) and evaluate his resources as insufficient (feeling underprepared, lacking confidence). The difference in their stress responses is entirely explained by differences in appraisal.

# 8. Coping Strategies

## 8.1 Approach and Avoidance Strategies

**Approach coping strategies** involve directly confronting or addressing the stressor. Examples include:

- Seeking expert advice or information about the problem
- Creating a structured action plan
- Problem-solving and brainstorming solutions
- Seeking social support (talking to friends, family, or professionals)
- Directly addressing a conflict through open communication
- Developing new skills relevant to the challenge

Approach strategies are generally associated with **better long-term mental health outcomes**. People who habitually use approach coping report fewer psychological symptoms and greater perceived control over their lives.

**Avoidance coping strategies** involve evading, minimising, or disengaging from the stressor. Examples include:

- Denial (refusing to acknowledge the problem exists)
- Distraction (turning attention to unrelated activities)
- Procrastination (delaying action on the stressor)
- Fantasy or wishful thinking
- Substance use (alcohol, drugs)
- Emotional disengagement (suppressing feelings about the stressor)

> **⚠️ Exam Alert:** A common misconception — and a frequent source of lost marks — is that avoidance coping is always maladaptive. This is **not true**. Avoidance strategies can be **appropriate and effective** when the stressor is genuinely **uncontrollable**. For example, while waiting for medical test results (a stressor you cannot influence), distraction is a reasonable and healthy coping strategy. Dwelling on the results would increase anxiety without changing the outcome. The key principle is **context-specific effectiveness** (Section 8.2).

## 8.2 Context-Specific Effectiveness

**Context-specific effectiveness** means that a coping strategy's appropriateness depends on the **match between the strategy and the situation**. No strategy is universally good or universally bad — effectiveness depends on context.

The general principle is:

- **Approach strategies** are generally more effective when the stressor is **controllable** — when the person can actually do something to address or change the situation (e.g. studying for an exam, seeking treatment for a health condition, having a difficult conversation to resolve a conflict).
- **Avoidance strategies** are sometimes more appropriate when the stressor is **uncontrollable** — when the person cannot change the situation and must endure it (e.g. waiting for surgery, grieving the death of a loved one, experiencing an unavoidable natural disaster). In these situations, approach strategies may increase frustration without reducing stress.

However, even when avoidance is initially appropriate, **excessive long-term reliance** on avoidance strategies is associated with poorer mental health outcomes. A person who uses avoidance to cope with every stressor — including controllable ones — is likely to experience increased anxiety, unresolved problems, and a diminished sense of control.

## 8.3 Coping Flexibility

**Coping flexibility** is the ability to **modify, adjust, or switch coping strategies based on changing situational demands**. It involves three components:

1. **Awareness:** Recognising that the current strategy is ineffective or no longer appropriate
2. **Discontinuation:** Stopping the ineffective strategy
3. **Replacement:** Implementing a different, more suitable strategy

High coping flexibility is associated with **better mental health outcomes** — people who can adapt their coping approach to match changing circumstances experience less distress and recover more quickly from stressful events (Kato, 2012). Low coping flexibility — rigidly persisting with the same coping strategy regardless of whether it is working — is associated with depressive symptoms and prolonged distress.

**Example:** A student initially copes with exam stress by creating a detailed study schedule (approach strategy). Midway through the exam period, she realises she is burning out and switches to incorporating regular exercise and social activities (a blend of approach and avoidance). After the exams, she uses distraction and leisure activities to decompress (avoidance strategy). Her ability to recognise when each strategy is working, stop when it isn't, and try something different demonstrates high coping flexibility.

> **⚠️ Exam Alert — The 2024 Coping Flexibility Disaster:** In 2024, Q5c asked about the relationship between coping flexibility and mindfulness meditation. Only **1% of students scored full marks**, and 43% scored zero. The key insight students missed: **mindfulness meditation emphasises sustained, non-judgemental acceptance of present-moment experience** — it inherently involves committing to a single, consistent approach (acceptance) rather than switching strategies. This makes it **potentially incompatible** with coping flexibility, which requires the ability to abandon an approach and try something different. High-scoring responses explained this tension: mindfulness can be one valuable tool within a flexible coping repertoire, but mindfulness *as a sole strategy* limits flexibility. If a question asks you to evaluate coping flexibility in relation to another concept, think about whether the other concept **supports or constrains** the ability to switch strategies.

# 9. Bringing It All Together: Stress as a Psychobiological Process

The word "psychobiological" in the heading of this Area of Study is not decorative — it tells you exactly how the VCAA wants you to think about stress. Stress involves both **psychological** processes (perception, appraisal, emotional responses) and **biological** processes (sympathetic activation, cortisol release, neurotransmitter changes, gut-brain communication). Neither dimension alone captures the full picture.

This is why the study design includes **both** a biological model (GAS) and a psychological model (the Transactional Model). Each captures a different aspect of the stress experience. GAS explains the body's universal physiological response to chronic stress. The Transactional Model explains why individuals differ in their stress responses based on cognitive appraisal. The fight-flight-freeze response explains immediate, acute threat responses. The gut-brain axis reveals a newly understood pathway through which stress affects — and is affected by — the body's largest collection of microorganisms.

For the exam, the highest-scoring responses will be those that can move fluently between biological and psychological explanations, selecting the model or mechanism most appropriate to the specific scenario, and acknowledging the limitations of each. This is the essence of a psychobiological approach — understanding that mind and body are not separate systems but deeply interconnected ones.

# Why It Matters

Stress is one of the most universally experienced psychological phenomena. Every student sitting the VCE Psychology exam has experienced stress — possibly about that very exam. But stress is far more than a feeling. It is a complex **psychobiological process** — meaning it involves both psychological and biological mechanisms working together. Your body's stress response evolved to keep you alive in genuinely dangerous situations. The problem is that the same system that helped your ancestors escape predators now activates when you receive a difficult email, sit in traffic, or think about an upcoming deadline.

This chapter connects directly to everything you learned in Chapter 1. The nervous system structures you studied — the sympathetic and parasympathetic divisions, neurotransmitters, the enteric nervous system — are the very systems that produce and regulate the stress response. Stress is, in effect, the nervous system in action under pressure.

This is also where the exam gets hard. The 2023 and 2024 exams both tested stress content extensively, and student performance was notably poor on questions requiring **evaluation** of stress models and **application** to novel scenarios. In 2023, 74% of students scored zero when asked to evaluate the Transactional Model's ability to explain heart-rate changes during nightmares. In 2024, only 1% of students scored full marks on a question about coping flexibility and mindfulness meditation. These are not content failures — they are application failures. Students knew the models but could not use them to reason about unfamiliar situations. This chapter will teach you not only what the models say, but how to evaluate what they can and cannot explain.

# 1. Understanding Stress: Stressors and Stress Responses

## 1.1 Defining Stress

**Stress** is a state of physiological and psychological arousal produced by internal or external stressors that are perceived as challenging or exceeding a person's resources to cope. Notice that this definition has both a biological component (physiological arousal) and a psychological component (perception of challenge) — this is why stress is described as a **psychobiological process**.

A **stressor** is any stimulus — internal or external — that causes a stress response. Stressors are not inherently positive or negative; what matters is how the individual perceives and responds to them.

**Eustress** is a **positive** stress response characterised by feelings of motivation, excitement, alertness, and focused energy. It occurs when a person perceives a stressor as manageable and potentially beneficial. Examples include the nervous excitement before a sporting final, the anticipation of starting a new job, or the energising pressure of a tight deadline that motivates productive work.

**Distress** is a **negative** stress response characterised by anxiety, tension, anger, helplessness, or feeling overwhelmed. It occurs when a person perceives a stressor as threatening, harmful, or exceeding their capacity to cope. Examples include dread before a difficult exam, grief after a relationship breakdown, or chronic anxiety about financial pressures.

> **⚠️ Exam Alert:** Eustress and distress are types of **responses**, not types of stressors. The same stressor can produce eustress in one person and distress in another, depending on their perception and resources. A job interview might produce eustress in a well-prepared, confident candidate and distress in someone who feels underprepared. If a question asks you to distinguish eustress from distress, focus on the **response** (how the person feels and functions), not on the stressor itself.

## 1.2 Internal and External Stressors

**Internal stressors** originate **within the individual**. They include negative self-talk ("I'm going to fail"), perfectionism, illness, chronic pain, fatigue, hormonal changes (such as those during puberty or menstruation), and unrealistic personal expectations.

**External stressors** originate from the **environment**. They can be categorised by intensity:

- **Daily pressures (hassles):** traffic, homework deadlines, minor disagreements, running late
- **Life events:** moving house, starting a new school, getting married, having a baby
- **Acculturative stress:** challenges of adapting to a new culture, including language barriers, discrimination, and loss of familiar social support
- **Major stressors:** serious illness or injury, violent crime, death of a loved one, divorce
- **Catastrophes:** natural disasters (such as the 2019–20 Black Summer bushfires, the 2022 Victorian floods), pandemics, war

## 1.3 Physiological and Psychological Stress Responses

Stress produces responses across three dimensions:

**Physiological responses** are the body's automatic biological reactions: increased heart rate and blood pressure, rapid breathing, sweating, muscle tension, pupil dilation, suppressed digestion, and elevated cortisol and adrenaline levels. These are mediated primarily by the sympathetic nervous system and the HPA axis (covered in Section 2).

**Psychological responses** encompass changes in cognition, emotion, and behaviour:

- *Cognitive effects:* difficulty concentrating, racing thoughts, indecisiveness, mental "blanking," impaired memory
- *Emotional effects:* anxiety, irritability, anger, sadness, helplessness, fear
- *Behavioural effects:* withdrawal from social activities, changes in eating or sleeping patterns, substance use, aggression, procrastination

# 2. The Fight-Flight-Freeze Response and the Biology of Stress

## 2.1 The Fight-Flight-Freeze Response

The **fight-flight-freeze response** is the body's involuntary, immediate reaction to a perceived acute threat. It is controlled by the autonomic nervous system and occurs without conscious decision-making.

The process begins when the **amygdala** (a small, almond-shaped structure deep in the brain's temporal lobe that processes emotions, especially fear) detects danger — either from direct sensory input or from the brain's interpretation of a situation. The amygdala signals the **hypothalamus**, which acts as the command centre, activating the **sympathetic nervous system**.

### Fight or Flight

When the sympathetic nervous system becomes dominant, the **adrenal medulla** (the inner part of the adrenal gland, sitting on top of each kidney) releases **adrenaline** (also called epinephrine) and **noradrenaline** (norepinephrine) into the bloodstream. These hormones produce rapid physiological changes designed to prepare the body for vigorous physical action:

- Heart rate and blood pressure increase (to pump more blood to muscles)
- Breathing rate increases and bronchioles dilate (to take in more oxygen)
- Blood is redistributed from the digestive system to skeletal muscles (to fuel movement)
- Pupils dilate (to improve visual acuity)
- Sweating increases (to cool the body during exertion)
- Blood glucose levels rise (to provide energy)
- Digestion is suppressed (energy is redirected to more immediately useful functions)

These changes prepare the body to either confront the threat (**fight**) or escape from it (**flight**).

### Freeze

The **freeze response** occurs when the threat seems overwhelming, inescapable, or when the person cannot determine whether to fight or flee. Contrary to what many students assume, freeze is **not simply more sympathetic activation**. Instead, the **parasympathetic nervous system overrides the sympathetic nervous system**, producing:

- Body movements and vocalisations stop (tonic immobility)
- Heart rate decelerates
- Blood pressure drops
- Muscles may collapse or become rigid
- The person may feel "frozen," unable to move or speak

The freeze response has evolutionary value: in predator-prey situations, remaining completely still can avoid detection. In modern contexts, it is commonly experienced during traumatic events, sudden shock, or overwhelming fear.

> **⚠️ Exam Alert:** The 2023 examiner report noted that many students incorrectly identify the sympathetic NS as dominant during freeze. This is wrong. The **sympathetic NS** is dominant during **fight and flight**. The **parasympathetic NS** is dominant during **freeze**. If a question asks which division is dominant during the freeze response, the answer is parasympathetic.

## 2.2 Cortisol and the Chronic Stress Response

The fight-flight-freeze response is designed for **acute** (short-term) threats. But what happens when the stressor doesn't go away? When stress becomes chronic, a different biological system takes over: the **HPA axis**.

The **HPA axis** (Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Adrenal axis) is a slower, sustained hormonal stress response pathway:

1. The **hypothalamus** releases corticotropin-releasing hormone (**CRH**)
2. CRH stimulates the **anterior pituitary gland** to release adrenocorticotropic hormone (**ACTH**)
3. ACTH travels via the bloodstream to the **adrenal cortex** (the outer layer of the adrenal gland — distinct from the adrenal medulla)
4. The adrenal cortex releases **cortisol**

**Cortisol** is the body's primary chronic stress hormone. Its functions include increasing blood sugar levels, enhancing the metabolism of fats and proteins for energy, suppressing the immune system, and retarding tissue repair. In the short term, these effects are adaptive — they keep the body fuelled and functioning under pressure.

However, **prolonged cortisol exposure** (from chronic stress) has serious harmful effects:

- Hypertension and cardiovascular disease
- **Hippocampal shrinkage** — impairing memory formation and retrieval
- Weakened immune function — increasing vulnerability to illness
- Disrupted sleep patterns
- Increased vulnerability to anxiety and depression
- Weight gain (particularly abdominal fat)

> **⚠️ Exam Alert — Critical Distinction:** Students frequently confuse adrenaline and cortisol. Remember: **adrenaline** comes from the adrenal **medulla** and is released during **acute** stress (immediate, short-term). **Cortisol** comes from the adrenal **cortex** and is released during **chronic** stress (sustained, long-term). Both can operate simultaneously, but they serve different temporal functions and come from different parts of the adrenal gland. In 2024, students confused cortisol depletion with adrenaline depletion when answering questions about the exhaustion stage of GAS. Use the correct hormone for the correct context.

# 3. The Gut-Brain Axis (GBA)

## 3.1 What Is the Gut-Brain Axis?

The **gut-brain axis (GBA)** is a **bidirectional communication link** between the brain and the gastrointestinal (GI) tract. "Bidirectional" means that signals travel in both directions — the brain influences the gut, and the gut influences the brain.

The VCAA study design includes the GBA as "an area of emerging research" — this means you need to understand the basic principles, know its significance for stress and the nervous system, and recognise that research in this area is still developing. You do not need to have memorised every molecular pathway, but you do need to understand the key mechanisms and be able to discuss them in relation to stress.

The GBA communicates through multiple pathways:

**The vagus nerve** is the primary neural link between the gut and brain. It is the longest cranial nerve in the body, running from the brainstem to the abdomen. Approximately **80–90% of vagus nerve fibres are afferent** (carrying information from the gut to the brain), while only **10–20% are efferent** (carrying information from the brain to the gut). This means the gut sends far more information to the brain than the brain sends to the gut.

**The HPA axis** provides a hormonal link — cortisol released during stress alters the composition of gut microbiota, which in turn affects gut function and can feed back to influence brain function.

**The immune system** provides another link — approximately 70% of the body's immune tissue is gut-associated. Gut inflammation can trigger immune signals that affect brain function, mood, and behaviour.

**Microbial metabolites** are chemicals produced by gut bacteria that influence brain function. Gut microbiota produce neurotransmitters including approximately **95% of the body's serotonin**, as well as GABA and dopamine. These chemicals influence mood, anxiety, sleep, and cognitive function.

## 3.2 Gut Microbiota and Stress

**Gut microbiota** refers to the trillions of microorganisms (bacteria, fungi, viruses) living in the gastrointestinal tract. A **diverse and balanced** gut microbiota is associated with better mental wellbeing and reduced stress reactivity. **Dysbiosis** — an imbalance in gut microbiota composition — is linked to increased stress, anxiety, depression, and gastrointestinal disorders such as irritable bowel syndrome (IBS).

The relationship between gut microbiota and stress is bidirectional:

- **Stress affects the gut:** Chronic stress and elevated cortisol alter the composition of gut microbiota, reducing diversity and promoting the growth of harmful bacteria. This can lead to digestive problems, inflammation, and further disruption of normal gut function.
- **The gut affects stress:** Changes in gut microbiota composition alter the production of neurotransmitters and immune signals that travel to the brain, influencing mood, anxiety, and stress reactivity. An unhealthy gut can amplify the stress response; a healthy gut can dampen it.

### Research in Focus: Key GBA Studies

**Sudo et al. (2004):** Researchers raised mice in completely germ-free environments (no gut bacteria at all). These germ-free mice showed an **exaggerated HPA stress response** — they produced significantly more cortisol when exposed to stress compared to normal mice. When the germ-free mice were then colonised with *Bifidobacterium infantis* (a beneficial bacterium), their stress response **normalised**. This demonstrated that gut microbiota is essential for normal stress regulation.

**Bravo et al. (2011):** Mice fed the probiotic *Lactobacillus rhamnosus* showed altered GABA receptor expression in the brain and **reduced stress-related behaviour** (less anxiety, less depressive-like behaviour). Crucially, these effects **disappeared entirely when the vagus nerve was severed** — confirming that the vagus nerve is the key communication pathway through which gut bacteria influence brain function.

> **⚠️ Exam Alert:** The gut-brain axis is a flagship topic of the 2023–2027 study design — it was brand new when this study design was introduced. In 2023, the GBA was the focus of the **10-mark extended response** (the highest-value question on the exam), asking students to analyse a study on psychobiotic diets and discuss the role of gut microbiota in stress. In 2024, GBA content appeared in short-answer questions about the vagus nerve and nutritional intake as a protective factor. Expect this topic to continue appearing in future exams. Key points to master: bidirectional communication, the vagus nerve's predominantly afferent role (80–90% gut-to-brain), and the evidence that gut microbiota influence neurotransmitter production and stress reactivity.

# 4. Selye's General Adaptation Syndrome (GAS)

## 4.1 The Model

Hans Selye (1907–1982), often called the "father of stress research," developed the **General Adaptation Syndrome (GAS)** as a **three-stage biological model** of the body's response to stress. His key insight, drawn from research at McGill University in the 1930s, was that the body responds to **all stressors** with the **same general physiological pattern**, regardless of the specific nature of the stressor. He called this the **non-specificity principle** — whether the stressor is physical (extreme cold, injury) or psychological (exam pressure, relationship conflict), the body's biological response follows the same three-stage sequence.

Selye's original 1936 research involved exposing rats to a range of noxious stimuli (cold, surgical injury, excessive exercise, toxic injections). Despite the different stressors, all rats developed the same triad of physiological changes: **enlarged adrenal cortex**, **shrunken thymus gland and lymph nodes** (immune suppression), and **stomach ulcers**.

### Stage 1: Alarm Reaction

The alarm reaction is the body's initial response to the stressor. It has two sub-phases:

**Shock phase:** The body's first awareness of the stressor. Resistance **drops below normal** as the body is caught unprepared. Blood pressure and body temperature fall temporarily. There may be a brief loss of muscle tone. This phase is typically very brief.

**Counter-shock phase:** The body mobilises its defences. The **sympathetic nervous system** activates, triggering the fight-flight-freeze response. Adrenaline is released from the adrenal medulla. Resistance **rises rapidly above normal** as the body prepares to deal with the threat. Heart rate, blood pressure, and breathing rate increase. Blood sugar is mobilised for energy.

### Stage 2: Resistance

If the stressor persists beyond the initial alarm, the body enters the resistance stage. The intense arousal of the alarm stage diminishes, but the body maintains a **sustained level of physiological arousal above normal**. The adrenal cortex releases **cortisol** to maintain energy supplies and repair minor damage. Non-essential physiological processes (digestion, growth, reproduction, immune function) are suppressed to conserve resources for coping with the stressor.

During the resistance stage, the person may appear to be coping well — they are functioning and adapting to the stressor. However, the body's resources are being gradually depleted. If the stressor is removed during this stage, the body returns to homeostasis (normal functioning). If the stressor continues, the body moves to the third stage.

### Stage 3: Exhaustion

If the stressor persists long enough, the body's resources become **depleted**. Resistance **falls below normal** — even below the initial pre-stress baseline. The person becomes highly vulnerable to:

- **Physical disorders:** hypertension, cardiovascular disease, stomach ulcers, chronic fatigue
- **Immune suppression:** increased susceptibility to infections and illness
- **Psychological disorders:** anxiety, depression, burnout
- In Selye's original animal experiments, prolonged exposure ultimately resulted in **death**

### The Standard GAS Graph

You should be able to draw and interpret the standard GAS graph from memory:

- **X-axis:** Time
- **Y-axis:** Level of Resistance to Stress
- **Horizontal dashed line:** Normal resistance level

The curve shows the characteristic "dip-rise-plateau-fall" shape:

1. **Shock** — resistance dips below normal (brief)
2. **Counter-shock** — resistance rises sharply above normal
3. **Resistance** — resistance plateaus above normal (sustained)
4. **Exhaustion** — resistance falls below normal and continues declining

## 4.2 Evaluating the Explanatory Power of GAS

The VCAA study design uses the phrase "explanatory power" three times across U3 AOS1, and the 2023 examiner report explicitly stated that students should practise evaluating the explanatory power of all stress models. **Evaluating** means making a judgement about how well the model explains a phenomenon — not just describing what the model says.

### Strengths of GAS

- **First model to systematically link chronic stress to disease.** Before Selye, the connection between prolonged stress and physical illness was not well understood. GAS provided a framework for understanding how stress "gets under the skin."
- **Rich physiological detail.** GAS describes specific biological mechanisms (adrenal activation, cortisol release, immune suppression) that can be measured and verified.
- **Strong laboratory evidence.** Selye's research demonstrated consistent, replicable physiological responses across different stressors and different organisms.
- **Explains non-specificity.** The model accounts for why very different stressors (physical injury, emotional trauma, illness) can produce similar physical outcomes (hypertension, ulcers, immune compromise).

### Limitations of GAS

- **Ignores individual differences.** GAS assumes everyone responds to stress in the same biological way, in the same sequence. In reality, people vary enormously in their stress responses based on personality, past experience, perceived control, and social support.
- **Ignores psychological factors.** GAS is a purely biological model — it does not account for cognitive appraisal, perception, or meaning-making. Two people facing the same stressor may experience very different stress responses depending on how they **interpret** the situation, but GAS cannot explain this.
- **Based primarily on animal research.** Selye's foundational research used rats, raising questions about the generalisability of findings to humans, who have more complex cognitive processes.
- **Assumes a linear progression.** GAS implies that everyone who faces chronic stress will progress through alarm → resistance → exhaustion in order. In practice, people may move back and forth between stages, or never reach exhaustion.
- **Does not explain eustress.** GAS treats all stress as leading toward negative outcomes (exhaustion, disease). It cannot account for positive stress responses (eustress) or situations where stress enhances performance.

> **⚠️ Exam Alert:** When the exam asks you to **evaluate** GAS, you must make a judgement — not just list strengths and limitations. A strong evaluation connects the model's features to the specific scenario in the question and explains **why** the model can or cannot adequately account for what is described. For example: "GAS has strong explanatory power for Mei's situation because her chronic work stress has persisted for months, and her symptoms (fatigue, frequent illness, high blood pressure) are consistent with the exhaustion stage, where prolonged cortisol exposure depletes bodily resources and suppresses immune function. However, GAS cannot explain why Mei experiences more severe stress than her colleague facing the same workload, because it does not account for individual differences in cognitive appraisal."

# 5. Lazarus and Folkman's Transactional Model of Stress and Coping

## 5.1 The Model

Richard Lazarus (1922–2002) and Susan Folkman (b. 1938) proposed that stress is not simply a stimulus or a response — it arises from the **transaction** (interaction) between an individual and their environment. The model's core principle is:

**Stress occurs when perceived demands exceed perceived resources to cope.**

Unlike GAS, which focuses on universal biological responses, the Transactional Model focuses on **cognitive appraisal** — the mental processes by which a person evaluates a situation and their ability to deal with it. This is why different people can experience very different levels of stress in response to the same stressor.

The model involves two stages of appraisal:

### Primary Appraisal

**Primary appraisal** is the person's initial evaluation of the **significance** of a situation — "What does this mean for me?" There are three possible outcomes:

- **Irrelevant:** The situation has no relevance to the person's wellbeing. No stress response occurs. (Example: hearing that a road you never use is closed for repairs.)
- **Benign-positive:** The situation is perceived as favourable or positive. No stress response occurs, and the person may experience positive emotions. (Example: receiving an unexpected compliment from a teacher.)
- **Stressful:** The situation is perceived as potentially harmful, threatening, or challenging. This triggers further evaluation. Stressful appraisals are further categorised as:
  - **Harm/loss:** Damage has already occurred. (Example: you have already failed an exam.)
  - **Threat:** There is potential for future harm or loss. (Example: you are about to sit a difficult exam you feel underprepared for.)
  - **Challenge:** There is potential for growth, gain, or mastery. (Example: you see the exam as an opportunity to demonstrate your knowledge.)

### Secondary Appraisal

**Secondary appraisal** is the person's evaluation of their **coping resources and options** — "What can I do about this?" The person assesses both:

- **Internal resources:** Confidence, knowledge, skills, past experience, physical fitness, emotional resilience
- **External resources:** Money, time, social support, access to information or professional help

If the person judges that their resources are **sufficient** to meet the demands, a stress response may be minimal or absent, and the person selects an appropriate coping strategy. If the person judges that the demands **greatly exceed** their resources, a significant stress response occurs.

> **⚠️ Exam Alert — Scope Limitation:** The VCAA study design specifies "primary and secondary appraisal only." **Reappraisal** (a third step where the person re-evaluates the situation after initial coping attempts) is NOT required by the study design. Students who include reappraisal are wasting time and word count on non-examinable content. Stick to primary and secondary appraisal only.

### Standard Flowchart

You should be able to draw this from memory:

```
Stressor → PRIMARY APPRAISAL
             ├── Irrelevant → No stress
             ├── Benign-positive → No stress
             └── Stressful
                  ├── Harm/loss
                  ├── Threat        → SECONDARY APPRAISAL
                  └── Challenge       ├── Resources sufficient → Coping strategy
                                      └── Resources insufficient → STRESS RESPONSE
```

## 5.2 Evaluating the Explanatory Power of the Transactional Model

### Strengths

- **Explains individual differences.** This is the model's central strength. Because stress depends on subjective appraisal, the model can explain why two people facing the same stressor experience different levels of stress — one may appraise it as a challenge while the other appraises it as a threat.
- **Focuses on controllable factors.** Appraisal and coping resources can be modified through psychological interventions (e.g. cognitive behavioural therapy, stress management training). This gives the model practical therapeutic value.
- **Based on human research.** Unlike GAS, the Transactional Model was developed from research with human participants, enhancing its relevance to human stress experiences.
- **Led to effective interventions.** The model underpins many evidence-based stress management approaches, including cognitive restructuring (changing how a person appraises a situation) and resource-building (developing coping skills and social support).

### Limitations

- **Cannot explain stress that occurs without conscious appraisal.** This is the model's most critical limitation. Some stress responses occur **automatically**, without any cognitive evaluation. Examples include: heart-rate increases during nightmares (the person is asleep and unable to consciously appraise), startle responses to sudden loud noises, and physiological stress responses in infants (who lack the cognitive capacity for appraisal).
- **Overlooks physiological responses.** The model focuses on psychological processes (appraisal, perception) and does not adequately explain the biological mechanisms of stress (cortisol release, immune suppression, autonomic activation).
- **Difficult to test experimentally.** Appraisal is a subjective, internal process that cannot be directly observed or precisely measured. Research relies on self-report, which is vulnerable to inaccuracies.
- **Assumes sequential appraisal.** The model implies that primary appraisal occurs before secondary appraisal. In reality, these processes often occur simultaneously or in rapid alternation, making them difficult to distinguish.

> **⚠️ Exam Alert — The Nightmare Question:** In the 2023 exam, Q7b asked students to evaluate the Transactional Model's appropriateness for explaining heart-rate increases during nightmares. **74% of students scored zero.** The mistake was describing what primary and secondary appraisal would look like in a nightmare, rather than evaluating whether the model **could explain the phenomenon at all**. The correct approach: the Transactional Model has **poor explanatory power** for nightmares because (1) the person is **asleep** and therefore unable to engage in **conscious cognitive appraisal**, and (2) heart-rate changes are **physiological responses** controlled by the autonomic nervous system, which the model (as a psychological model) does not adequately address. **Evaluate** means judge — does this model work here, or doesn't it? Don't describe the model when asked to evaluate it.

# 6. Comparing GAS and the Transactional Model

Understanding the differences between these two models is essential for exam questions that ask you to compare, contrast, or evaluate them. The table below captures the key distinctions:

| Feature | GAS (Selye) | Transactional Model (Lazarus & Folkman) |
|---------|-------------|----------------------------------------|
| **Focus** | Universal biological response | Individual cognitive appraisal |
| **Type of model** | Biological | Psychological |
| **Language** | Biological: cortisol, adrenaline, alarm, resistance, exhaustion, adrenal cortex, immune suppression | Psychological: appraisal, perceived threat/challenge/harm, coping resources, perceived demands |
| **Individual differences** | Cannot explain — assumes the same response for everyone | Central feature — explains why people respond differently to the same stressor |
| **Evidence base** | Primarily animal research (rats) | Primarily human research |
| **Strengths** | Explains physiology of chronic stress; links stress to disease | Explains individual variation; identifies controllable factors |
| **Limitations** | Ignores psychology and individual differences | Cannot explain stress without conscious appraisal; ignores physiology |
| **Best at explaining** | Chronic stress leading to physical illness; physiological stress responses | Individual variation in stress responses; why perception matters |
| **Poor at explaining** | Why two people respond differently to the same stressor | Automatic physiological responses; stress in sleep, infants, or unconscious states |

> **⚠️ Exam Alert — Match Your Language to the Model:** The examiners penalise students who mix biological language with the psychological model or vice versa. When discussing GAS, use biological terms: cortisol, adrenaline, adrenal cortex, adrenal medulla, alarm/resistance/exhaustion, sympathetic activation, immune suppression. When discussing the Transactional Model, use psychological terms: appraisal, perceived threat/challenge/harm, coping resources, perceived demands. In the 2023 exam, only 38% of students correctly answered a multiple-choice question because the distractors used Lazarus & Folkman language in options about GAS. If you can keep the vocabularies separate, you have a significant advantage.

# 7. A Framework for Evaluating Explanatory Power

The VCAA expects you to be able to evaluate the explanatory power of GAS, the Transactional Model, and the fight-flight-freeze response across **diverse scenarios** — not just the familiar textbook examples. The 2023 examiner report explicitly recommended that students practise this skill throughout the year.

Here is a simple four-question framework you can apply to any scenario:

1. **Can this model explain what is happening in this scenario?**
2. **What can it explain well?** (Which specific features of the scenario does it account for?)
3. **What can't it explain?** (Which features of the scenario fall outside the model's scope?)
4. **Why?** (What is it about the model's focus — biological vs psychological — that creates this limitation?)

### Practice: Applying the Framework

**Scenario A: Workplace Burnout**
*Mei has been working 60-hour weeks for eight months. She is exhausted, frequently sick, and has developed high blood pressure.*

- **GAS:** Strong explanatory power. Mei's symptoms align with the exhaustion stage — prolonged stress has depleted her resources, leading to immune suppression (frequent illness), cardiovascular effects (high blood pressure), and fatigue. GAS's non-specificity principle explains why chronic work stress produces physical symptoms.
- **Transactional Model:** Moderate explanatory power. It can explain why Mei experiences stress (she may appraise her workload as exceeding her resources), but it cannot explain the specific physiological symptoms. It could explain why a colleague in the same role might cope better (different appraisal of demands vs resources), but not Mei's high blood pressure or immune suppression.

**Scenario B: Nightmares**
*Twelve-year-old Zac experiences increased heart rate and sweating during a nightmare about being chased.*

- **GAS:** Moderate explanatory power. The alarm reaction's activation of the sympathetic NS (increased heart rate, sweating) matches Zac's symptoms. However, GAS does not distinguish between conscious and unconscious experiences of stress, and it is unclear whether the full three-stage model applies to a brief nightmare.
- **Transactional Model:** Poor explanatory power. Zac is asleep and unable to engage in conscious cognitive appraisal. His heart-rate increase and sweating are involuntary physiological responses, not products of deliberate evaluation of demands versus resources. The model fundamentally requires conscious appraisal, which cannot occur during sleep.
- **Fight-flight-freeze:** Strong explanatory power. The amygdala can activate the sympathetic NS during a nightmare based on dream content, triggering fight-or-flight responses (increased heart rate, sweating) without conscious appraisal.

**Scenario C: Exam Preparation**
*Two students, Aisha and Ben, are preparing for the same VCE Psychology exam. Aisha feels motivated and focused; Ben feels overwhelmed and anxious.*

- **GAS:** Poor explanatory power. Both students face the same stressor, but their responses are completely different — one experiences eustress, the other distress. GAS predicts the same biological response for both and cannot account for this individual variation.
- **Transactional Model:** Strong explanatory power. Aisha may appraise the exam as a **challenge** (potential for mastery) and evaluate her resources as sufficient (good preparation, strong study skills). Ben may appraise it as a **threat** (potential for failure) and evaluate his resources as insufficient (feeling underprepared, lacking confidence). The difference in their stress responses is entirely explained by differences in appraisal.

# 8. Coping Strategies

## 8.1 Approach and Avoidance Strategies

**Approach coping strategies** involve directly confronting or addressing the stressor. Examples include:

- Seeking expert advice or information about the problem
- Creating a structured action plan
- Problem-solving and brainstorming solutions
- Seeking social support (talking to friends, family, or professionals)
- Directly addressing a conflict through open communication
- Developing new skills relevant to the challenge

Approach strategies are generally associated with **better long-term mental health outcomes**. People who habitually use approach coping report fewer psychological symptoms and greater perceived control over their lives.

**Avoidance coping strategies** involve evading, minimising, or disengaging from the stressor. Examples include:

- Denial (refusing to acknowledge the problem exists)
- Distraction (turning attention to unrelated activities)
- Procrastination (delaying action on the stressor)
- Fantasy or wishful thinking
- Substance use (alcohol, drugs)
- Emotional disengagement (suppressing feelings about the stressor)

> **⚠️ Exam Alert:** A common misconception — and a frequent source of lost marks — is that avoidance coping is always maladaptive. This is **not true**. Avoidance strategies can be **appropriate and effective** when the stressor is genuinely **uncontrollable**. For example, while waiting for medical test results (a stressor you cannot influence), distraction is a reasonable and healthy coping strategy. Dwelling on the results would increase anxiety without changing the outcome. The key principle is **context-specific effectiveness** (Section 8.2).

## 8.2 Context-Specific Effectiveness

**Context-specific effectiveness** means that a coping strategy's appropriateness depends on the **match between the strategy and the situation**. No strategy is universally good or universally bad — effectiveness depends on context.

The general principle is:

- **Approach strategies** are generally more effective when the stressor is **controllable** — when the person can actually do something to address or change the situation (e.g. studying for an exam, seeking treatment for a health condition, having a difficult conversation to resolve a conflict).
- **Avoidance strategies** are sometimes more appropriate when the stressor is **uncontrollable** — when the person cannot change the situation and must endure it (e.g. waiting for surgery, grieving the death of a loved one, experiencing an unavoidable natural disaster). In these situations, approach strategies may increase frustration without reducing stress.

However, even when avoidance is initially appropriate, **excessive long-term reliance** on avoidance strategies is associated with poorer mental health outcomes. A person who uses avoidance to cope with every stressor — including controllable ones — is likely to experience increased anxiety, unresolved problems, and a diminished sense of control.

## 8.3 Coping Flexibility

**Coping flexibility** is the ability to **modify, adjust, or switch coping strategies based on changing situational demands**. It involves three components:

1. **Awareness:** Recognising that the current strategy is ineffective or no longer appropriate
2. **Discontinuation:** Stopping the ineffective strategy
3. **Replacement:** Implementing a different, more suitable strategy

High coping flexibility is associated with **better mental health outcomes** — people who can adapt their coping approach to match changing circumstances experience less distress and recover more quickly from stressful events (Kato, 2012). Low coping flexibility — rigidly persisting with the same coping strategy regardless of whether it is working — is associated with depressive symptoms and prolonged distress.

**Example:** A student initially copes with exam stress by creating a detailed study schedule (approach strategy). Midway through the exam period, she realises she is burning out and switches to incorporating regular exercise and social activities (a blend of approach and avoidance). After the exams, she uses distraction and leisure activities to decompress (avoidance strategy). Her ability to recognise when each strategy is working, stop when it isn't, and try something different demonstrates high coping flexibility.

> **⚠️ Exam Alert — The 2024 Coping Flexibility Disaster:** In 2024, Q5c asked about the relationship between coping flexibility and mindfulness meditation. Only **1% of students scored full marks**, and 43% scored zero. The key insight students missed: **mindfulness meditation emphasises sustained, non-judgemental acceptance of present-moment experience** — it inherently involves committing to a single, consistent approach (acceptance) rather than switching strategies. This makes it **potentially incompatible** with coping flexibility, which requires the ability to abandon an approach and try something different. High-scoring responses explained this tension: mindfulness can be one valuable tool within a flexible coping repertoire, but mindfulness *as a sole strategy* limits flexibility. If a question asks you to evaluate coping flexibility in relation to another concept, think about whether the other concept **supports or constrains** the ability to switch strategies.

# 9. Bringing It All Together: Stress as a Psychobiological Process

The word "psychobiological" in the heading of this Area of Study is not decorative — it tells you exactly how the VCAA wants you to think about stress. Stress involves both **psychological** processes (perception, appraisal, emotional responses) and **biological** processes (sympathetic activation, cortisol release, neurotransmitter changes, gut-brain communication). Neither dimension alone captures the full picture.

This is why the study design includes **both** a biological model (GAS) and a psychological model (the Transactional Model). Each captures a different aspect of the stress experience. GAS explains the body's universal physiological response to chronic stress. The Transactional Model explains why individuals differ in their stress responses based on cognitive appraisal. The fight-flight-freeze response explains immediate, acute threat responses. The gut-brain axis reveals a newly understood pathway through which stress affects — and is affected by — the body's largest collection of microorganisms.

For the exam, the highest-scoring responses will be those that can move fluently between biological and psychological explanations, selecting the model or mechanism most appropriate to the specific scenario, and acknowledging the limitations of each. This is the essence of a psychobiological approach — understanding that mind and body are not separate systems but deeply interconnected ones.

# Key Terms Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Acculturative stress** | Stress resulting from adapting to a new culture, including language barriers, discrimination, and loss of familiar social support |
| **Acute stress** | Short-term stress in response to an immediate threat or demand; associated with adrenaline release from the adrenal medulla |
| **Adrenal cortex** | The outer layer of the adrenal gland; releases cortisol during chronic stress via the HPA axis |
| **Adrenal medulla** | The inner part of the adrenal gland; releases adrenaline and noradrenaline during acute stress via the sympathetic nervous system |
| **Adrenaline (epinephrine)** | Hormone released by the adrenal medulla during acute stress; produces rapid physiological changes for fight-or-flight |
| **Approach coping strategies** | Strategies that directly confront or address the stressor, such as problem-solving, seeking advice, or taking action |
| **Avoidance coping strategies** | Strategies that evade, minimise, or disengage from the stressor, such as denial, distraction, or procrastination |
| **Chronic stress** | Prolonged, sustained stress; associated with cortisol release from the adrenal cortex and negative health outcomes |
| **Context-specific effectiveness** | The principle that a coping strategy's appropriateness depends on the match between the strategy and the situation |
| **Coping flexibility** | The ability to modify, adjust, or switch coping strategies based on changing situational demands |
| **Cortisol** | The body's primary chronic stress hormone; released by the adrenal cortex via the HPA axis; suppresses immune function and increases blood sugar |
| **Distress** | A negative stress response characterised by anxiety, tension, helplessness, or feeling overwhelmed |
| **Dysbiosis** | An imbalance in gut microbiota composition; associated with increased stress, anxiety, and gastrointestinal disorders |
| **Enteric nervous system (ENS)** | The mesh-like network of neurons in the gastrointestinal tract walls; part of the gut-brain axis |
| **Eustress** | A positive stress response characterised by motivation, excitement, alertness, and focused energy |
| **Exhaustion (GAS Stage 3)** | The stage of GAS where bodily resources are depleted, resistance falls below normal, and vulnerability to illness increases |
| **External stressors** | Stressors originating from the environment, such as daily pressures, life events, or catastrophes |
| **Fight-flight-freeze response** | The body's involuntary, immediate reaction to perceived acute threat; fight and flight involve sympathetic dominance; freeze involves parasympathetic dominance |
| **General Adaptation Syndrome (GAS)** | Selye's three-stage biological model of the body's non-specific physiological response to stress: alarm reaction, resistance, exhaustion |
| **Gut-brain axis (GBA)** | The bidirectional communication link between the brain and gastrointestinal tract, operating through the vagus nerve, HPA axis, immune system, and microbial metabolites |
| **Gut microbiota** | The trillions of microorganisms living in the gastrointestinal tract; influence neurotransmitter production, immune function, and stress reactivity |
| **HPA axis** | The Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Adrenal axis; the hormonal pathway for chronic stress, culminating in cortisol release from the adrenal cortex |
| **Internal stressors** | Stressors originating within the individual, such as negative self-talk, illness, pain, or fatigue |
| **Non-specificity (GAS)** | Selye's principle that the body responds to all stressors with the same general physiological pattern |
| **Primary appraisal** | The initial evaluation of a situation's significance: irrelevant, benign-positive, or stressful (harm/loss, threat, challenge) |
| **Resistance (GAS Stage 2)** | The stage of GAS where the body sustains elevated physiological arousal above normal to cope with a persistent stressor |
| **Secondary appraisal** | The evaluation of coping resources and options — whether the person's resources are sufficient to meet the demands |
| **Stress** | A state of physiological and psychological arousal produced by stressors perceived as challenging or exceeding resources to cope |
| **Stressor** | Any stimulus — internal or external — that causes a stress response |
| **Transactional Model of Stress and Coping** | Lazarus and Folkman's model proposing that stress arises from the transaction between an individual and their environment, through cognitive appraisal |
| **Vagus nerve** | The longest cranial nerve; the primary neural link between the gut and brain; approximately 80–90% afferent (gut-to-brain) |
